Van Insulation: What Actually Works (And What's a Waste of Money)

Infinity Vans April 2026 ~1,500 words

Why Insulation Is the Foundation of Every Livable Van

You can drop $10,000 on a lithium battery bank, a roof-mounted solar array, and a diesel heater that could warm a small house. None of it matters if your van sweats like a cold beer on a summer afternoon. Insulation isn't glamorous. It won't show up in your Instagram reel. But it is the single most consequential decision you'll make in your entire build.

Get it right and your van stays comfortable in July heat and January cold. Get it wrong and you spend every morning wiping condensation off your walls, watching mold creep into your cabinetry, and wondering why your heater runs constantly. This isn't a small problem. It's a structural one.

Before you buy a roll of anything, read this. Because most of what you've heard about van insulation (from forums, from YouTube builds, from well-meaning Reddit threads) is either incomplete, outdated, or optimized for someone else's van in someone else's climate.

The Real Enemy: Moisture, Not Temperature

Here's the thing most builders get wrong from the start. They treat insulation as a temperature problem. It's not. It's a moisture management problem that happens to involve temperature.

When warm, humid air hits a cold surface (your bare steel van walls), it condenses. Water appears. Over time, that water rusts your van from the inside out, grows mold inside your walls, and destroys whatever insulation you've installed. You never see it happening. That's what makes it dangerous.

Your job isn't just to add R-value. Your job is to keep warm air from reaching cold metal, or to eliminate the cold metal entirely. Two builders can use the same product and get wildly different results based on how they approach the moisture problem. Understanding how different insulation types manage heat transfer is useful context before choosing one for your van. Strategy matters more than material selection.

The goal isn't to stop moisture. It's to never let the conditions for condensation exist in the first place.

Ventilation is part of this equation too. A good roof fan (Maxxair or Fan-Tastic) running on exhaust while you cook or sleep actively removes moisture-laden air from the van. Insulation and ventilation are partners, not substitutes for each other.

Sound Deadening vs. Insulation: Stop Confusing These

Sound deadening (products like Dynamat or FatMat) reduces resonance and road noise. It does almost nothing for thermal performance, with an R-value somewhere around R-0.5. Thermal insulation is what keeps heat in and cold out. Spending hundreds covering every panel with sound deadening, then wondering why your heater runs constantly, is one of the most common (and expensive) mistakes in a van build. Sound deadening has its place on the floor and roof, but it's a supplement, not a strategy.

Common Insulation Materials: Real-World Performance

Spray Foam

Closed-cell spray foam has excellent R-value (R-6 to R-7 per inch), adheres directly to metal, and creates an air barrier. The problem: DIY kits are messy, temperature-sensitive, and unforgiving. Once it's in, it's in. There's also a real concern that foam applied over damp metal seals in rust rather than preventing it. Professional application solves the installation issues but adds significant cost. The hype outpaces the real-world results for most self-builders.

Polyiso Rigid Foam Board

Polyiso runs R-6 to R-6.5 per inch, lightweight and easy to cut. It's excellent for flat surfaces (floor, ceiling, flat wall sections), but fitting rigid board around curved van geometry takes patience. Any gap becomes a thermal bridge and condensation point. Budget extra time for fitting and gap-sealing.

XPS (Extruded Polystyrene)

Similar to polyiso in application, XPS runs around R-5 per inch. Slightly lower R-value, but more moisture-resistant and more forgiving in damp environments. Widely available and cheap. The same fitting challenges apply, but it's a legitimate, cost-effective approach for builders willing to do careful work.

Mineral Wool (Rockwool)

Fire-resistant and vapor-permeable at R-3.7 to R-4 per inch. The real-world issue: it absorbs moisture readily, and in a sealed van wall cavity, wet Rockwool loses R-value and stays damp. Vapor permeability only helps if moisture has somewhere to go. Use it with care and good vapor management, not as a default choice.

Thinsulate (3M SM600L)

Easy to install (cut and stuff), vapor-permeable, non-toxic, at around R-3.8 per inch. The problem: it's expensive per R-value compared to rigid foam alternatives. And like Rockwool, vapor permeability only helps if moisture can actually escape. It's a good material for complex curved sections and rib cavities, but using it everywhere because it's easy is leaving performance on the table. Full-coverage Thinsulate at full price might be on the list of things you think you need but don't.

Fiberglass Batt

Don't. Fiberglass absorbs and holds moisture. In a van wall cavity, wet fiberglass becomes a rust accelerator, and R-value drops significantly when wet. It's the wrong tool for this job, designed for stick-built houses with drywall vapor barriers, not metal vehicles.

Thermal Bridging: The Hidden Performance Killer

You can insulate every square inch of your van walls and still have significant heat loss. Every metal rib, every screw connecting your interior to the outer shell is a direct pathway for heat to escape. Metal conducts heat roughly 300 times more efficiently than air, so the steel ribs running every few inches are actively undermining your foam board while it does its job. This is why R-value ratings are misleading in a van context: system-level thermal performance, accounting for all the bridging points, is what actually determines how warm or cold you'll be.

Addressing thermal bridging means creating a continuous thermal break between your interior wall surface and the van's metal structure. That's harder to achieve with piecemeal insulation than most builders anticipate. It's also one of the strongest arguments for integrated wall systems.

The Case for Integrated Panel Systems

Building a great insulation system from scratch is possible. But it requires time, precision, and a high tolerance for iteration. A piecemeal approach creates a piecemeal wall: foam board here, Thinsulate there, vapor barrier taped around a wire run, and a wall panel sitting slightly off the ribs. These gaps and variations are where moisture problems incubate. This is the core argument behind the flat pack philosophy: integrated systems designed specifically for the van they go into outperform assembled-in-the-field solutions at almost every level.

The Bottom Line

The van conversion industry benefits from making insulation feel complicated. Real-world performance comes down to a few principles: continuous coverage with no exposed metal, thermal bridge management, a real moisture strategy (not just plastic sheeting and hope), and matching your material to the surface. Rigid board for flat runs, flexible material for complex geometry, and system thinking that connects insulation to ventilation and your finished walls.

Insulation done right is invisible. You won't think about it when you wake up in a 20-degree parking lot and your van is still warm. You won't think about it when you open your walls two years later and find clean, dry metal with no rust. You'll just be comfortable, and your build will be holding up.

Smart builders get this right the first time. If you're building with that mindset, take a look at what's available in the van build kits section. Not because you need to buy a kit. Because understanding how integrated systems solve the problems you're about to spend a weekend wrestling with is worth fifteen minutes of your time.

Build it once. Build it right.
