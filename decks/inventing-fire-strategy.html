01 / Where things stand

You already built the hard part. It's stalling on production.

Inventing Fire with AI has a real concept, a credible host, a book, a website, and distribution across every major platform. What it doesn't have is a system to keep it running.

Billed as weekly, the show has shipped roughly four episodes across nine months. The cause is simple: production is a second job sitting on top of running an AI consulting firm. Recording, editing, clipping, writing, coordinating field footage, publishing everywhere. That's where good shows quietly die.

The opportunity here has nothing to do with starting something new. It's about removing the friction so the brand you've already started can actually compound.

The goal goes beyond "a podcast." We're building a content engine that makes you the name business operators trust when they need to understand AI, published consistently enough that the market can rely on it.

03 / The show architecture

A two-host conversation, with the street woven in.

You're at your best in conversation, not solo, so the structure is built for chemistry and for clips, in the spirit of Scott and Mark Learn To: two people who actually know their stuff, having a real conversation, teaching something useful without taking themselves too seriously. A man-on-the-street segment adds texture and keeps the show grounded in how real people meet AI.

Anchor

Host: Rich

The expert POV. Funny, blunt, shock-and-awe. 30 years of credibility behind every take.

Foil

Co-host

The conversational counterweight (potentially your brother) who keeps it loose, real, and unscripted.

Texture

Man on the street

Field interviews that ground each episode in how real people actually meet AI.

You already have a videographer who can capture the man-on-the-street footage when needed. Our job is to take that raw material, the main conversation plus field segments, and assemble it into a tight, repeatable episode structure that holds attention from segment to segment and produces natural clip moments by design.

04 / The content engine

Two recordings a month become a month of content.

A biweekly cadence (2 episodes/month) is the sustainable rhythm: frequent enough to build an audience, light enough to protect your calendar. The leverage is in what each episode becomes after the recording.

Per episode, you get:

1Full episode. Edited audio + video, conversation plus field segment assembled into one tight cut
4–6Short-form clips. Cut for YouTube Shorts, Instagram, LinkedIn, X
∞Supporting assets. Show notes, transcript, thumbnails, episode artwork

Across a month, two recordings yield two full episodes plus 8–12 short-form clips and a steady drip of written assets. The "tell it like it is" moments, where you say the part vendors won't, are exactly what we hunt for in the edit, because those are what get forwarded in Slack threads and shared to leadership teams. The show funds its own distribution.

05 / Discovery & distribution

Your audience searches before they subscribe.

A business owner trying to figure out AI doesn't open a podcast app. They search. YouTube is the second-largest search engine in the world, and "where AI breaks for [their business]" is exactly the high-intent query your content answers.

  • Video-first publishing to YouTube, optimized titles and thumbnails so episodes surface for the searches your audience is already making.
  • Short-form everywhere. Clips distributed to the platforms where operators actually spend time, each one a doorway back to the full show.
  • Owned channels reinforced. The site, blog, and email list you already have become a flywheel instead of standalone efforts.

Consistency is what makes any of this work. Algorithms reward cadence; audiences reward reliability. The single biggest unlock for discovery has nothing to do with tactics. It comes down to publishing every two weeks without fail.
