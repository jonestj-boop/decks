Why This Strategy

A plain-English walkthrough of how this plan was built, what it's designed to do, and what to expect.

How We Picked These 120 Keywords

We started from Coastal Crate's product catalog, the 4 main supplement categories you sell, and the assessment we ran on coastalcrate.com. From there we ran 18 seed keywords through SEMrush's Keyword Strategy Builder, which expanded into ~12,400 related US keywords across the four pillars below.

Every keyword in this plan was scored on three things: monthly search volume (will real pet parents find it?), keyword difficulty (can we realistically rank?), and brand fit (does it match Coastal Crate's catalog and audience?). We picked the 120 keywords that win on all three.

The Four Content Pillars

Every article belongs to one of four pillars. Each maps directly to a category in your catalog and the questions pet parents actually ask before buying:

Joint & Mobility · 32 articles

Glucosamine, MSM, hip and joint, senior mobility, arthritis support. Highest single-keyword volume in your category — this is the anchor pillar.

Calming & Anxiety · 32 articles

Calming chews, separation anxiety, storm and travel calmers, CBD comparisons. High-emotion search behavior — these convert exceptionally well.

Gut Health & Digestion · 28 articles

Probiotics, prebiotics, digestive enzymes, sensitive stomach support. Pet parents are increasingly searching for gut health — this pillar is growing 40% YoY.

Skin, Coat & Allergies · 28 articles

Allergy chews, omega-3, fish oil, skin and coat care. Strong cross-sell into joint and gut categories — same buyers, different intent moments.

Why Most Articles Are Awareness-Stage (And That's the Right Move)

The plan splits roughly 73% awareness, 25% comparison, and 2% decision. That mirrors how pet parents actually search. Most queries are research before buying, fewer are direct purchase decisions. We don't fight that.

  • Awareness articles bring new readers in from Google. They link to your product collection and category pages, moving readers down the funnel after they arrive on your blog.
  • Comparison articles ("Glucosamine vs MSM," "Best Calming Chews," "Cosequin vs Glucosamine") capture readers who already know what they want and are picking between options. These convert at 3–5x the rate of awareness content.
  • Decision articles are scarce because true transactional searches in pet supplements are mostly product names ("Cosequin," "Adequan") that Amazon and Chewy own. We don't compete there. Decision-stage conversion happens through your product detail pages and email flows, not SEO.

Why Some Keywords Are Hard to Rank, and Why We Still Picked Them

About 14 of 120 articles target keywords with a difficulty score above 35 (still moderate, not hard — pet supplement SERPs are less competitive than enterprise SaaS). We include them for two reasons:

  • Defensive must-rank topics. For keywords like "best probiotics for dogs," "calming chews for dogs," and "dog joint supplements," if Coastal Crate doesn't show up in those searches, that's a brand-presence problem regardless of difficulty. We have to compete there.
  • Volume plays. Keywords like "dog melatonin" (27,100 mo) and "coconut oil for dogs" (27,100 mo) send meaningful traffic even at page 2. The math works at that scale.

The other 106 articles target moderate or easy keywords (71 of them at KD 25 or below). These are the fast wins that should start ranking in months 2 to 4.

What This Plan Deliberately Excludes

Several big-volume keywords didn't make the cut. They're either off-brand, regulated, or unwinnable:

  • Veterinary diagnosis content: "dog seizures," "dog kidney failure," "dog cancer." These SERPs are owned by VCA, AKC, and PetMD. Not winnable, and the content carries medical-claim liability for a supplement brand.
  • Prescription medication topics: "Apoquel for dogs," "Cytopoint side effects." Off-label and regulatory minefield. We focus on natural supplement alternatives instead.
  • Generic dog food content: "best dog food," "raw vs kibble." Owned by Chewy, BarkBox, and Wirecutter. Wrong vertical for a supplements brand.
  • Pure breed-specific content: "best food for golden retriever," "labradoodle joint problems." We cover breed-specific angles only when they map to a clear product fit (e.g., "Large Breed Joint Supplements") — generic breed roundups don't drive conversions.

What to Expect, Month by Month

Months 4 to 6

Months 6 to 12
Authority builds; defensive head terms start ranking

SEO is a 6 to 12 month investment, not a 30-day campaign. The first articles published in Month 1 won't drive meaningful traffic until Month 3 or later — that's just how Google indexing works. By Month 6 the easy-win articles should be generating consistent organic traffic, and the harder defensive head terms (joint supplements, calming chews, probiotics) will start moving onto page 1.

For faster revenue: SEO content is the long-game foundation. Pair it with paid acquisition (Meta + Google Performance Max) on your highest-intent product pages. That's where the immediate revenue comes from. The blog content reduces paid CAC over time as organic traffic compounds and your retargeting pool grows.

How Each Article Is Built

Every article in this plan goes through the same ClusterMagic production process: deep research on the target keyword and competing top-10 results, a draft written in Coastal Crate's brand voice, vet-cited sources, SEO optimization (title tags, meta descriptions, schema, internal linking), human review, and publication on your CMS as a draft. Each article is 1,200–2,000 words, includes 2–3 internal links to related articles in the same pillar, and links to at least one product collection page.

The companion deliverable: the keyword research spreadsheet (coastal-crate-pet-co-keyword-research.xlsx) contains the full ~12,400 keywords surfaced from SEMrush, with the 120 selected for this plan flagged. Use it to identify content overflow opportunities, future article topics, and to run lookups against any specific keyword you have questions about.

How to Read This Plan

Primary Keyword: the main search term each article will be optimized for. Each keyword expands into dozens of related long-tail searches during the writing process.

Monthly Searches: how many people search this exact term each month (SEMrush, US database).

Difficulty (KD): how hard it is to rank on page 1 of Google. 0 to 20 Very Easy · 21 to 35 Easy · 36 to 50 Moderate · 51 to 70 Hard.

Pillar: which content cluster the article belongs to. Articles within a pillar link to each other and to a central "hub" post for maximum SEO authority.

Funnel Stage: Top means awareness and education. Middle means comparison and research. Bottom means ready to buy.
