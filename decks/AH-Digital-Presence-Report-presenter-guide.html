Before you start: mindset

This is a different kind of document than a CRO/site audit

There's no equivalent recorded walkthrough call for this report the way there was for the Ayoub CRO audit, so this guide is built from the document itself, not from a transcript. Read it once end to end before presenting; the tone here is more diplomatic and less "here's what's broken" than a conversion audit.

The report's own closing section names the tension directly: "Nothing in this document is a request to operate outside brand guidelines or reduce headquarters' role... What we are asking for is the infrastructure to be discoverable in the cities where Adriana Hoyos has showrooms." Lead with that framing, not with the technical findings.

  • This is a business case for internal advocacy, not a sales pitch to a skeptical prospect. The people in the room may need to take this exact argument to their own HQ. Give them language they can reuse, not just findings they have to translate themselves.
  • Never let this read as "HQ is the problem." The report explicitly pre-empts that: "This is not a criticism of headquarters. It is a structural gap that the brand's growth in the US market depends on solving." Repeat that line if the tone in the room turns defensive of HQ.
  • Lead with the brand, not the deficiencies. Executive summary line to open on: "The core issue is not brand quality." Adriana Hoyos has the press, the clientele, the name. The gap is purely digital infrastructure for new markets.
  • Bo Concept is a proof point, not a threat. It's used throughout as evidence this is solvable (a direct comparable brand already solved it in Georgetown), not to imply AH is losing to a lesser competitor. Keep that framing consistent.
  • Money framing lands with this audience. Shevy in particular responds to dollars-and-decisions language over jargon (confirmed pattern from the Ayoub CRO walkthrough). Section 06's paid-ads cost argument (irrelevant landing pages raise Google's Quality Score penalty, which raises cost-per-click) is probably the single most persuasive point in the whole report for him specifically. Don't bury it, it's near the end of section 6.
Pre-call checklist
  • Report open and full-screen, or this printed/PDF'd for reference alongside the live URL.
  • Know the one non-negotiable fact upfront: Google Ads is already going to run for the DC opening one way or another. Without a DC page, that spend is structurally wasted, this isn't a future risk, it's a current-plan cost.
  • Have effort/cost ballparks ready for at least Option 1 (the minimum ask), since that's the one requiring no HQ workflow change and is the easiest yes.
  • Confirm before the call who in the room can actually approve HQ-level changes (site access, content workflow). If it's not that person, your job is arming the US team to make the case upward, not closing HQ directly.
  • Know your one ask: approval to build the Option 1 DC location page before the showroom opens, independent of any decision on Options 2 or 3.

The 90-second story (the spine of the whole report)

Adriana Hoyos has the brand: press in Architectural Digest, Elle Decor, and Robb Report, a loyal clientele, a recognized name. What it doesn't have is a website built to introduce that brand to people who don't already know it. 89% of all US traffic lands on the homepage via someone typing "Adriana Hoyos" directly, and Washington DC returns zero organic search results for the brand today, not one keyword. A direct comparable, Bo Concept, already ranks for 7,800+ keywords AH doesn't touch, including a dedicated Georgetown DC store page capturing 1,000 monthly searches for "contemporary furniture stores near me" alone. Underneath the SEO gap sits a structural one: a 48-hour HQ approval window and no site access mean the US team can't even respond to local opportunity, and it directly taxes paid ads too, a DC ad with nowhere relevant to land gets a lower Quality Score and costs more per click. The ask isn't independence from HQ, it's the minimum digital infrastructure to be findable in the cities where the brand already has showrooms.

The three beats, in order:

  • 1. What the data shows (sections 01–04): traffic only reaches people who already know the name; DC has zero presence; a direct competitor has already solved this exact problem nearby.
  • 2. Why it's happening (sections 05–06): technical SEO gaps compound with an operational structure (HQ approval, no access) that blocks fixing them, and that same structure directly taxes paid ad spend.
  • 3. What to do about it (section 07): three scaled options, a minimum first step before the DC opening, and a closing ask framed as partnership, not independence.

05Technical Findings: how the site is built works against it

On screen

6 issues in plain language: 28 H1 tags on the homepage (should be 1), no LocalBusiness/schema markup anywhere, keyword cannibalization (20+ pages competing for "adriana hoyos design studio," 47 URLs for "adriana hoyos dining chairs"), auto-generated/missing meta descriptions sitewide, no individual page per US showroom, backlink authority declining 12 months (Authority Score flat at 27, referring domains down ~900 → 657, ~27% decline, and most press links are nofollow).

Say

"Six structural things are working against the site. The homepage sends Google 28 competing headline signals instead of one, so Google can't tell what the page is really about. There's no structured data anywhere telling Google 'this is a business, here's the address and hours,' so showroom info reads as plain text, not business data Maps can use. Dozens of pages compete against each other for the same searches instead of one strong page winning, one keyword alone has 47 different AH pages competing for it. Search snippets are auto-generated because no one's written custom descriptions. There's no individual page per showroom. And even the strong press, Architectural Digest, Elle Decor, Robb Report, mostly links back as 'nofollow,' so it doesn't pass ranking benefit, and overall linking domains have dropped about 27% in a year."

Quick cues
  • 28 H1 tags on the homepage (best practice: 1)
  • Zero LocalBusiness/schema markup anywhere on the site
  • Keyword cannibalization: 47 URLs competing for one dining-chairs term
  • No custom meta descriptions on any reviewed page
  • No individual page for any US showroom
  • Authority Score flat at 27; referring domains down ~27% in 12 months; top press links are nofollow
Data → action

Keep this section brief and matter-of-fact, it's the most "audit-sounding" part of the report and risks tipping the tone into criticism. Land it, then move quickly into section 06's structural/operational explanation, that's the more persuasive half.

If asked: is this a rebuild?

"No. Most of this is additive: schema markup, meta descriptions, consolidating competing pages, and new location pages. Nothing here proposes changing the site's design or brand presentation."

06Content and Access: the structure blocks local marketing, including paid

On screen

Comparison table: 48-hour HQ content approval vs. same/next-day need; no pricing/discounts allowed; no dedicated showroom URL; only approved brand assets; no direct site access for the US team; GBP management unconfirmed for DC. Then 4 findings on how the same constraints directly cost money on paid ads: irrelevant landing pages, higher CPCs from lower Quality Score, no conversion tracking without site access, no ability to run time-sensitive campaigns.

Say (this is the section to slow down on)

"This is where the SEO gap and an operational one meet. Right now, any US-specific content needs 48-hour HQ approval, there's no dedicated URL for any showroom, and the US team has no direct site access. That alone blocks local marketing. But it also has a direct, current cost on paid ads, which matters even more for a showroom opening. Every DC-targeted ad today points to the generic homepage, because there's no DC page to send it to. Google's Quality Score penalizes an irrelevant landing page, which raises cost-per-click, so you're already paying more for the same clicks than a competitor with a dedicated page would. And without site access, conversion tracking can't even be installed, so there's no way to prove which ad spend is working. None of this is a criticism of headquarters, it's a structural gap the brand's US growth depends on closing, and it's actively taxing whatever paid budget is already planned for the opening."

Quick cues
  • 48-hr approval, no pricing in content, no dedicated URL, no site access, GBP unconfirmed
  • Direct ad cost: irrelevant landing page → lower Quality Score → higher CPC
  • No site access = no conversion tracking = can't prove what's working
  • Time-sensitive campaigns (opening, launches, events) structurally can't be supported
  • Explicitly: "not a criticism of headquarters... a structural gap"
Data → action

This is the section most likely to land with a CMO-minded audience, because it's dollars, not rankings. If time is short, this and section 01 are the two you protect.

If asked: are we already wasting ad spend today, or is this future risk?

"Both, but it's not hypothetical. If any DC-targeted paid campaign runs before a dedicated page exists, and most openings run some kind of paid push, it's already paying an avoidable markup on every click and generating no usable performance data. This is a current-plan cost, not a someday risk."

07Recommendations: three paths, and the ask

On screen

Three scaled options: 01 Minimum Ask — dedicated location pages within the existing site (e.g. adrianahoyos.com/locations/washington-dc/), full HQ oversight retained. 02 Recommended — a US market subfolder (adrianahoyos.com/us/) with an expedited same/next-day content workflow, HQ still approves brand-level material. 03 Highest Potential — a dedicated adrianahoyos.us site. Closing callout: the minimum step before the DC opening. Final section: "The Ask for This Conversation: Partnership, Not Independence."

Say

"Three options, scaled by investment and how much workflow changes at HQ. Option one is the floor: a dedicated page per US showroom inside the existing site, same domain, same HQ approval process, just a page that exists and is built so Google can read it as a real location. Option two is what we'd recommend if there's appetite for it: a US section of the site with a faster, same or next-day publishing path for local content, HQ still approves anything brand-level. Option three is the highest ceiling, a fully separate US domain, the model a lot of international luxury brands use market to market. All three keep HQ in full control of brand identity and visual standards, none of them is a request for independence. The one thing I'd ask you to walk away with today: before the DC showroom opens, get sign-off on at minimum a dedicated DC location page, adrianahoyos.com/locations/washington-dc/. Without it, every dollar of offline marketing sends people to a Google search that returns nothing."

Quick cues
  • Option 1 (Minimum Ask): location pages in the existing site, zero HQ workflow change
  • Option 2 (Recommended): US subfolder + faster local publishing, HQ still approves brand content
  • Option 3 (Highest Potential): dedicated adrianahoyos.us domain
  • All three: HQ keeps full brand control
  • The one ask: DC location page live before the showroom opens
Data → action

Don't ask the room to pick between all three today. Ask for the Option 1 minimum as a standalone yes, since it doesn't require any HQ policy change and can move independently of the bigger Option 2/3 decision.

If asked: how much does each option cost / take?

Have a ballpark ready for Option 1 specifically, it's the one most likely to get approved on this call. Option 2 and 3 are bigger asks; frame them as "here's the range, we'd scope precisely once there's directional interest" rather than a committed number.

If asked: can we just do Option 3 and skip the politics?

"You could go straight there if HQ is open to it, but Option 1 is designed specifically so it doesn't need that conversation at all, it's a page inside the site you already have, under the approval process you already have. We'd suggest getting that live first regardless of which longer-term path you choose."

If they push back: fast answers

"This makes it sound like we're blaming HQ."

"That's explicitly not the framing, the report says it directly: this is not a criticism of headquarters, it's a structural gap the US growth depends on closing. Every option keeps HQ in full control of the brand."

"We get plenty of walk-ins and referrals, why does online search matter?"

"Referrals are great, but they don't show up in a Google search for 'furniture showroom near me,' which 1,000 people a month search in DC alone. That volume goes entirely to Bo Concept right now. It's incremental, not a replacement for what's already working."

"We can't get HQ to change the approval process."

"Understood, that's exactly why Option 1 doesn't ask for that. It's a page inside the current site, under the current approval process. The workflow change is only needed for Option 2, and that can be a separate, later conversation."

"Is Bo Concept really a fair comparison?"

"It's the most direct one available, a comparable modern/contemporary furniture brand with an actual showroom in Georgetown, DC, so it's proof this is solvable in this exact market, not a hypothetical benchmark from a different category."

"Won't this hurt brand consistency or dilute the main site?"

"No, all three options are additive infrastructure, not a redesign. HQ retains approval on brand-level material in every version, including the recommended Option 2."

"Can this wait until after the showroom opens?"

"It can, but any paid or offline marketing that runs around the opening is actively taxed without it, wasted ad spend on Day 1, not a future problem. The location page should exist before the marketing push, not after."

"How is this different from the CRO audit you did?"

"The CRO audit is about converting the traffic the main global site already gets. This report is upstream of that, it's about whether the brand is even findable for a new local market and local search in the first place."

FAQs

"What's the one thing you need us to approve today?"

"Sign-off on Option 1: a dedicated DC location page, live before the showroom opens. It doesn't require any change to HQ's approval process."

"Do we need to pick between the three options now?"

"No. Option 1 can move independently and immediately. Options 2 and 3 are bigger, longer conversations that can happen on their own timeline."

"Who needs to sign off on this beyond this call?"

"Depends how much site/workflow access is needed. Option 1 likely only needs whoever approves standard site content today. Options 2 and 3 will need HQ leadership sign-off on process and, for Option 3, a new domain."

"What happens to our current paid ad plans if we do nothing?"

"They still run, but at a real cost penalty, higher cost-per-click from a low landing-page Quality Score, and no way to measure what's working since conversion tracking needs site access."

"How long would the minimum option take?"

"It's a single page within the existing site plus schema markup, the fastest of the three by a wide margin. Exact timeline depends on your standard content approval turnaround once we submit it." (Have a ballpark ready.)

"Will Google results change immediately once the page goes live?"

"Not instantly, but it's the prerequisite for everything else. Without the page and the schema data, DC rankings can never start building, no matter what else runs. With it, local rankings and Maps visibility can start accruing, and any paid campaign has somewhere relevant to send clicks."
