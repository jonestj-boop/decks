Core Web Vitals Benchmarks

How long until the main content, usually the hero image, actually appears. In plain terms: how fast the page looks loaded.
CLS (Cumulative Layout Shift) Good: <0.1 | Needs work: 0.1–0.25 | Poor: >0.25
How much the page jumps around while it loads, text and buttons shifting as images pop in. A stability score, not seconds.

Website Performance

Speed, mobile experience, and Core Web Vitals. The technical foundation everything else depends on.

40%
of visitors will leave if a page takes more than 3 seconds to load on mobile.

For a luxury brand, speed is part of the experience. A hero that arrives late undermines the same premium impression the photography is designed to create. Google also uses Core Web Vitals as a ranking signal, so slow pages surface lower in search.

  • Mobile performance score on key pages (Google rates under 50 as poor)
  • Largest Contentful Paint, how fast the main content appears
  • Cumulative Layout Shift, visual stability while loading
  • Unused JavaScript and duplicate plugins adding load weight
  • Media weight of image-heavy luxury pages
Pillar 1 — Findings

Website Performance

5 issues identified, 2 Critical. This is the highest-leverage pillar in the audit.

Critical
Homepage: mobile 39/100 with a 33-second LCP. The hero is invisible while the brand makes its first impression
The homepage weighs 6.6 MB across 160 requests. On mobile, Google measures 33.0s before the hero content paints; we observed the same live on desktop, where the nav renders and the hero area stays blank white for several seconds. Speed Index is 25.0s mobile. For a brand whose product is visual, the most expensive real estate on the site is loading last.
Critical
Contact page: mobile CLS 1.001 and 23.2s LCP on the primary lead-capture page
A CLS of 1.001 is four times the "poor" threshold. The contact form visibly shifts as the page assembles on mobile, inviting mis-taps and abandonment from prospects who are actively trying to get in touch. Mobile score is 30/100.
High
~950 KiB of unused JavaScript, including three overlapping analytics plugins
PageSpeed estimates 4.7s of savings from unused JS on the homepage and contact page. The site loads PixelYourSite, the official Facebook Pixel plugin, and Google Site Kit simultaneously, which duplicates tracking payloads on every page view.
High
Layout shift fails on every page tested (CLS 0.256 to 1.001 mobile)
Homepage 0.283, products 0.256, contact 1.001 on mobile. Images and lazy-loaded Divi modules render without reserved space, so the page jumps as content arrives.
High
About Us renders as a blank page for 8+ seconds
Scroll-triggered fade-in animations combined with slow hero media leave the About Us page looking completely empty on first paint. We loaded it twice and saw a white screen both times until scrolling forced content in. The brand's strongest storytelling page is effectively hidden.

UX & Navigation

Can your ideal customer profile find the right piece and move toward specifying it, or are they hitting walls?

2×
returning visitors convert at nearly double the rate of new ones. Big-ticket buyers rarely commit on the first visit; smooth navigation is what brings a specifier back to shortlist and order.

Adriana Hoyos serves interior designers, hospitality buyers, and end clients at once. Each needs to narrow a large catalog quickly: by collection, by room, by material, by what ships now.

  • Category pages: can users filter and sort a 39-product grid?
  • Footer as a navigation safety net
  • Related-product discovery on detail pages
  • Scroll behavior and wayfinding on the homepage
  • Store locator usability
Pillar 2 — Findings

UX & Navigation

4 issues identified. Search, notably, works well.

High
No filtering or sorting on category pages
The Sofa & Loveseats category presents 39 products in a single unfiltered grid. There is no way to narrow by collection, material, size, or Quick Ship availability, and no sort control. Competitor Artefacto offers Category, Indoor/Outdoor, and Order controls on every listing page.
High
Footer is a dead end: policies and social icons only
The homepage footer contains Terms of Use, Privacy Policy, BASC Policy, How We Work, and four grey social icons, no product categories, no contact details, no newsletter capture anywhere. The footer is also inconsistent page to page (product pages omit How We Work, add a fifth icon, and even credit a different copyright entity than the homepage).
Medium
"You May Also Like" renders empty and recommends across the wrong categories
On the Galapagos Sofa 100 page, the related-products section renders as a blank band. The underlying modules contain roughly 19 hidden items, mostly bedroom and dining pieces (dressers, nightstands, a bed, buffets, dining chairs) recommended on a living room sofa page, and every hidden card carries the same raw "Maximum quantity no defined" string.
High
Find Us map loads broken: 15+ seconds of grey, then a washed-out map centered on South Africa with no store pins
The store locator lists 99 shops worldwide, a genuine strength, but the map pane stayed an empty grey rectangle for 15+ seconds and then painted an extremely faint map centered on Gqeberha, South Africa, with no visible location markers. For a brand whose showrooms and retail partners drive revenue, a locator that cannot show a single pin is lost foot traffic on every visit.
What's working: on-site search returns relevant, well-presented product results ("Your Search, Your Results"), and the mega-menu's room-based product taxonomy is clear and logical.

Visual Messaging

Do the first three seconds tell a visitor what Adriana Hoyos is, who it serves, and why to choose it?

3 sec
is roughly how long a new visitor gives a homepage before deciding to stay or leave. The hero has to answer "what is this and why should I care" inside that window.

The photography and 3D product experience are genuinely premium. The gap is orientation: taglines speak in fashion language while the visitor still doesn't know if this brand sells to them, and polish details like raw system text leak into customer view.

  • Hero value proposition and audience orientation
  • CTA copy: specific and action-oriented, or generic?
  • Copy polish: system strings, heading structure
  • Product visuals: does the star asset load first?
Pillar 3 — Findings

Visual Messaging

5 issues identified, 1 Critical.

Critical
The hero never states what the brand is, who it serves, or why to choose it
Four auto-rotating slides carry taglines like "Explore our technologies", "LIVING IN FASHION", and "NOW AVAILABLE: NEW DESIGNS", with a generic "Discover More" CTA (the award slide's button reads "Galapagos Chair 501 Texture"). Nothing orients a first-time visitor: is this retail or trade-only, where is it sold, what makes it different? Combined with the slow hero load, the most valuable screen on the site communicates essentially nothing in the first seconds.
High
Raw system text leaks onto customer-facing product cards
Quick Ship product cards display "Maximum quantity no defined" and "Maximum quantity: 12" as visible card text. On a luxury site, one unpolished string undercuts an otherwise premium presentation.
Medium
27 H1 tags render on the homepage
The hero slider alone outputs 12 H1s (three responsive copies of each of the four slides), and 15 more render further down the page ("LATEST MAGIC", "INSPIRED COLLECTIONS", "A Legacy of Creative Vision", and others). Search engines see 27 competing page titles instead of one clear topic.
Medium
The 3D viewer blocks the primary product visual while it loads
Product pages open with a spinner and "Notice: Loading 3D viewer, please wait..." where the product photo should be. The 3D configurator is a real differentiator, but making it the default hero asset means the product is invisible during the seconds that matter.
Low
White hero text sits on light imagery on some slides
Slide text like "Discover More" approaches invisibility over pale interiors. (Credit where due: an accessibility widget is installed sitewide.)

Checkout Friction

For a trade brand, the "checkout" is the path from interested designer to approved account. Right now that path is a locked door.

70%
of started online forms are abandoned. Every unexplained gate, every extra field, and every unanswered "what happens next" pushes that number higher.

Gated trade pricing is a legitimate strategy; Bernhardt gates too. The difference is context. The best trade sites sell the program before asking for credentials. Adriana Hoyos asks for credentials without ever selling the program.

  • What a new prospect hits when they try to act (price, sample, quick ship)
  • Registration length, requirements, and expectation-setting
  • Is the purchase path ever explained?
  • Lead-capture forms: friction and follow-up clarity

Critical
Sample Request, the easiest first action for a new prospect, opens a bare login wall
Clicking SAMPLE REQUEST in the header produces a username/password modal with a "Create Account" button and zero explanation: who qualifies, what it costs, what happens after. A designer who just wants a fabric swatch is asked to register and upload a resale certificate first. This is the single most valuable top-of-funnel action on the site and it is fully gated.
High
Pricing is blurred with "If you want to see the price click here", and the click hits the same bare login
Product pages tease a blurred price next to a click-through that opens the unexplained login modal. Caracole shows a $10,030 sofa price publicly with a 20% welcome code. Bernhardt also gates pricing, but surrounds the gate with trade program marketing. Adriana Hoyos gates with no story at all.
High
Registration asks for 11 fields plus a resale certificate upload, then a manual review of unstated length
The signup form requires company, resale certificate number, certificate file upload, business type, and full address, and ends with "we will check your information and you will shortly receive a confirmation". No timeline, no statement of benefits, no indication of what "shortly" means. High-intent prospects are left in limbo at the moment of maximum motivation.
High
"How We Work", the only explanation of the purchase path, is buried in the footer
Nothing on product pages tells a prospect how buying actually works: trade vs. retail, showroom purchase, lead times, shipping. "How We Work" appears only in the homepage footer, and product-page footers omit it entirely.
High
Contact page sets no expectations and offers no phone or showroom details
The 6-field form ends in a generic SEND with no stated response time. The page lists team emails by region (a genuine plus) but no phone number, hours, or showroom address block. It is also the least stable page on mobile (CLS 1.001, see Pillar 1).
Why this pillar compounds: the trade-account system is the heart of the brand's digital funnel, and as the brand grows its retail footprint, this same account experience becomes the template for every new regional site and partner integration. Every fix here pays off more than once.

Urgency & Scarcity

Luxury doesn't discount, but it can still give buyers a reason to act now. Adriana Hoyos already has one: Quick Ship.

10–30%
conversion lift reported in case studies when authentic urgency signals are present at the point of decision. Authentic is the key word: real inventory, real dates, real programs.

Custom furniture usually means long lead times, so "pieces ready to ship in 6 weeks" is a genuinely differentiating promise. The data behind it (per-item quantities) already exists in the store. Both are currently undersold.

  • Is the Quick Ship promise visible where decisions happen?
  • Real inventory scarcity, surfaced in customer language
  • Collection launches and events as luxury-appropriate urgency
Pillar 5 — Findings

Urgency & Scarcity

3 issues identified. The raw material for authentic urgency already exists.

High
Quick Ship, the strongest urgency asset, is undersold
"Pieces ready to ship in 6 weeks" is a real differentiator in custom luxury furniture, where lead times often run 4 to 6 months. But the program's hero loads late, and homepage promotion is limited to one rotating slide.
High
Real scarcity is displayed as raw system data instead of a selling point
The Galapagos Dining Table 101 shows "Maximum quantity: 1". That is genuine, persuasive scarcity ("the last one, ready in 6 weeks") rendered as database output that reads like an error.
Medium
No launch moments, event dates, or seasonal hooks anywhere
There are no collection launch dates, market-week events, or showroom happenings on the site. Luxury brands create urgency editorially (limited collections, launch windows, event RSVPs) without ever discounting. Caracole, by contrast, runs welcome offers; Adriana Hoyos need not copy that, but currently offers no reason at all to act this month versus next.

⭐

De-risking & Social Proof

A designer specifying a five-figure piece for a client needs proof: who else trusts this brand, and what happens if something goes wrong?

15–20%
conversion lift case studies typically report when real customer proof is visible at the point of decision. For trade brands, that proof is testimonials, project case studies, and named clients.

Adriana Hoyos has the assets: a 40-year design legacy, hospitality installations, 99 points of sale, an award win, and a family story. Almost none of it is deployed where buying decisions happen.

  • Testimonials and case studies from designers and hospitality clients
  • Awards presented consistently and persistently
  • Press and third-party credibility
  • Policies a buyer can check before committing (shipping, warranty)
Pillar 6 — Findings

De-risking & Social Proof

5 issues identified.

High
Zero testimonials, case studies, or client names anywhere on the site
No designer quotes, no hospitality project case studies, no client logos. Competitor Artefacto carries TESTIMONIALS as a top-level navigation item. Adriana Hoyos has a Hospitality division and decades of installations, which is exactly the proof a specifier looks for, and none of it is on the site.
High
The LUXE RED award lives only in a rotating hero slide, with inconsistent naming
One slide says "Red Luxe Award winners 2026", another "Winner of LUXE RED Awards 2026". The award vanishes when the carousel rotates and appears nowhere else. Inconsistent naming also weakens searchability and credibility.
Medium
No press or media section
A luxury furnishings brand of this profile has trade-press coverage, but the site offers no "As seen in" strip or press page to borrow that credibility.
Medium
No shipping, warranty, or returns information available before login
Published policies are Terms of Use, Privacy, and BASC only. A prospect cannot answer basic de-risking questions: how does white-glove delivery work, what does the warranty cover, what if a piece arrives damaged?
Low
Instagram, the discovery channel for luxury interiors, is a small grey footer icon
Social proof by association (installs, designer tags, reels) stays invisible. The four social icons are low-contrast grey marks at the page bottom.
What's working: the About Us story (family-owned, designer-led, vertically integrated) is strong raw material, the contact page lists real named team members by region, and the 99-location Find Us locator signals global scale.

Execution Plan

Implementation Phases

Priority order based on conversion impact and ease of implementation on WordPress + Divi + WooCommerce.

Phase 1
Copy, content, and settings. Minimal developer time.
  • Rewrite the hero: one slide, clear value proposition, "Explore Collections" + "Trade Program" CTAs
  • Add explainer copy + approval timeline to the login/registration modal
  • Fix raw system strings on product cards ("Only 1 available" / "12 ready to ship")
  • Standardize the LUXE RED award name; add a persistent credential strip
  • Add response-time promise, phone, and showroom info to the contact page
  • Promote "How We Work" into main navigation
  • Rebuild the footer: categories, contact, trade links, newsletter
  • Fix homepage H1 structure (one H1, slides demoted)
Phase 2
Developer or plugin work. High impact.
  • Performance sprint: WebP heroes, preload LCP, WP Rocket caching, defer JS (homepage + contact first)
  • Fix contact-page layout shift (reserve space for form and embeds; target CLS < 0.1)
  • Consolidate analytics to one pixel manager; remove duplicate plugins
  • Guest sample-request form (no login required)
  • Registration: defer certificate upload, add SLA + benefits, instant confirmation email
  • WooCommerce filters + sort on category pages
  • Quick Ship badges on eligible product cards and detail pages
  • Static product photo first; 3D viewer on demand
  • Remove above-fold entrance animations (About Us and sitewide)
Phase 3
Ongoing optimization and content build-out.
  • Collect and publish designer + hospitality testimonials and case studies
  • Publish Shipping & Delivery and Warranty & Care pages
  • Press page + "As seen in" strip
  • Fix "You May Also Like" scoping (same collection) and lazy-render
  • Repair Find Us map initialization
  • Launch-moment calendar: collection premieres, market weeks, showroom events
  • Shoppable Instagram gallery on the homepage
  • Connect GA4 and build the trade-funnel dashboard (sessions → product views → sample requests → registrations)
  • Hero text scrim + contrast pass
Phase 1 alone (8 items, mostly copy and settings) sharpens the first impression and unblocks the trade funnel within days, before the heavier development work begins.
