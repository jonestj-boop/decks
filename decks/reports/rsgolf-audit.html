Executive Summary

The Big Picture

Red Stakes Golf has rebuilt itself into a genuinely compelling brand — only American-made golf simulator, 365-day money-back guarantee, 4.8-star ratings, major retail and brand partnerships, and a gamified platform that sets it apart from every competitor. The foundation is strong. The problem is conversion. Kevin shared the number directly: roughly 300 people add to cart, and a minuscule fraction (roughly 0.05%) actually make it to checkout. On a $3,000 average order, fixing that single drop-off is worth tens of thousands of dollars per month at current traffic levels — before scaling a dollar of ad spend.

Across all three channels, the theme is the same: trust and friction. The site has the proof points — it just isn't deploying them at the right moment in the buyer journey. Financing is nowhere. The 365-day guarantee is buried. Reviews are an aggregate number, not real stories. And ad traffic is landing on product pages that weren't built to convert cold visitors. These are solvable problems — and fixing them makes every other marketing investment more efficient.

300 add-to-carts with near-zero checkouts is not normal Shopify behavior. Industry average cart-to-checkout is 40–60%. Even a "struggling" store hits 15–20%. Critical Issues

1
No Financing Options on a $3,000–$12,100 Product Critical All Pages

The site offers no buy-now-pay-later or installment option anywhere — not in the hero, not on product pages, not in the cart, not in ads. For a product that starts at $3,000 and runs to $12,100, this is a massive barrier. The buyer thinking "I can't spend $7,100 today" never sees "from $197/month" and leaves. This is likely the single largest driver of cart abandonment.

Recommended Fix
  • Enable Shop Pay Installments immediately — it's built into Shopify, takes minutes to activate, and shows "Pay in 4" or monthly options automatically on product pages and checkout
  • Add an Affirm or Splitit app for longer-term financing (12–36 months) which is more appropriate for the $7K–$12K packages
  • Add "As low as $X/month" beneath every product price, and in the hero section of the homepage
  • Make financing a headline in paid ads, email campaigns, and retargeting — "Own a Red Stakes Golf Simulator from $197/month" kills the barrier before they even hit the site
  • Feature financing prominently in the cart with urgency: "Split this into 12 payments at checkout"
2
365-Day Guarantee Is Buried — Not a Hero Element Critical Homepage + PDPs

The 365-day money-back guarantee exists on the site — but it lives in a trust badge row, not as a headline feature. For a $3K–$12K product with a year-long return window, this is arguably the strongest purchase objection killer in the industry. It deserves its own section, its own language, and repeated placement everywhere the buyer hesitates: the hero, product pages above the fold, the cart, retargeting ads, and email flows.

Recommended Fix
  • Add a dedicated "Try it for a full year" section to the homepage — with a specific call-out of what "no questions asked" means in practice
  • On every product page, place a visually distinct guarantee block above the Add to Cart button, not below it
  • Rewrite the language: "Try for 365 days. If it's not the best money you've ever spent on your game, return it for a full refund. No questions, no hoops." beats "365-Day Money-Back"
  • Use the guarantee as the hook in retargeting ads and the subject line in abandoned cart emails
  • Create a dedicated guarantee landing page that ad traffic can be sent to for objection handling
3
No Real Social Proof on Product Pages Critical Product Pages

Product pages show "Avg. Customer Rating 4.8 / 5.0" — a number, not a story. There are no individual review quotes, no photos of real setups in customers' homes, no video testimonials, no "Kevin from Michigan" saying what the simulator did for his game. On a $3,000+ purchase, people don't trust aggregated scores — they trust people like them. The 4.8 rating is a strong asset that's being completely underutilized.

Recommended Fix
  • Install Okendo or Judge.me (Shopify review apps) to surface full review content, photos, and videos directly on product pages
  • Feature 3–5 pull quotes above the fold on each major product page — specifically ones that address the most common objections (setup difficulty, accuracy, space requirements)
  • Request UGC from existing customers: photos and short videos of their simulator rooms — these are gold for both the site and Meta retargeting ads
  • Add a "Real Setups" section or gallery page showing customer rooms — this also addresses the "will this fit in my space?" objection
  • On the homepage, replace the generic rating badge with 2–3 featured customer stories with names, locations, and specific outcomes

High Priority Issues

4
Hero CTA "Visit The Pro Shop" Is Too Generic High Homepage

The homepage hero CTA asks visitors to "Visit The Pro Shop" — a passive, low-intent phrase that doesn't match where a first-time buyer is mentally. Someone who just landed from a Google ad for "home golf simulator" wants to understand what they're buying and whether it's right for them, not browse a shop. The CTA should speak to desire, not a retail metaphor.

Recommended Fix
  • Test "Build Your Simulator" or "Find Your Setup" — language that promises a guided experience rather than a browsing experience
  • Add a secondary CTA for non-ready buyers: "Get a Free Consultation" or "Take the Quiz" — this captures leads who aren't ready to purchase and feeds the email/retargeting funnel
  • Align hero headline with the top-performing paid ad copy once you have data from the first month of campaigns
5
No Lead Capture for Non-Ready Buyers High Homepage + PDPs

A $3,000–$12,000 simulator is a 30–90 day decision. The vast majority of people who visit the site today are not ready to buy — they're researching. Right now, if they don't purchase, they're gone forever. There is no email capture, no quiz, no lead magnet, no consultation scheduler that gives them a reason to share their email and gives RSG a way to nurture them through the funnel. With 496K contacts already in the database, RSG clearly knows the value of an email list — the site just isn't building it.

Recommended Fix
  • Add an exit-intent popup: "Not ready to buy yet? Get our free guide — How to Build the Perfect Home Golf Room" in exchange for email
  • Build a "Simulator Finder Quiz" (3–5 questions: space size, budget, skill level) that results in a personalized recommendation — and captures email before showing results
  • Add a "Free Consultation" CTA that routes to a Calendly or similar — this directly feeds the sales team for high-ticket MOFU leads
  • Trigger an email sequence for quiz completers and consultation bookers that mirrors the 365-day guarantee, social proof, financing, and Made in USA angles
6
Product Page Structure Buries the Add to Cart High Product Pages

On the RSG One Launch Monitor product page, the "Get A Free Consultation" section (with team photo and phone number) appears before the Add to Cart button. This is the right concept — offering consultation for a high-ticket item — but the execution breaks the purchase flow. A buyer ready to add to cart has to scroll past a sales team section to do it. The consultation CTA should exist as a secondary option, not a gate before purchase.

Recommended Fix
  • Restructure PDP to: Price → Financing option → Trust badges (guarantee, Made in USA, rating) → Add to Cart → Features → Consultation CTA → Reviews → FAQ
  • Keep the consultation section — it's a good conversion tool — but move it below the fold as a "not sure? talk to us" option
  • Add a sticky "Add to Cart" bar that follows the user as they scroll (Shopify apps like Sticky Add to Cart can do this in minutes)
7
No Product Comparison Tool or "Help Me Choose" Experience High Collections + PDPs

RSG One vs RSG Pro. Launch Monitor vs Starter Edition vs Studio Edition. Prices from $3,000 to $12,100. A first-time buyer has no clear framework for understanding which product is right for them — and confusion kills conversions. Right now the site lists the products but doesn't guide the decision. The consultation CTA partially addresses this, but most buyers won't call — they'll bounce.

Recommended Fix
  • Add a comparison table to the Collections page showing key differences: tech specs, room size requirement, included courses, price, and ideal buyer ("Best for: Serious players wanting tour-level data")
  • Build the "Simulator Finder Quiz" mentioned in Issue #5 — this does double duty as lead capture and product guidance
  • Add "Most Popular" and "Best Value" labels to specific SKUs to reduce decision fatigue
  • Consider a dedicated "Compare Simulators" landing page that can be used as a paid ad destination

Medium Priority Issues

8
No Video on Product Pages Medium Product Pages

You're asking someone to spend $3,000–$12,000 on a physical product they can't touch, see in a store, or demo before buying. Images help — but video is the closest thing to a in-store experience. The gamified platform, the shot data, the setup process, the actual room size needed — none of this is communicated through static images alone.

Recommended Fix
  • Embed a 60–90 second product demo video at the top of each major PDP (YouTube or Vimeo embed works fine on Shopify)
  • Create a "Setup Walkthrough" video for the enclosure/studio editions — showing how to set it up in a real room reduces setup anxiety
  • Repurpose customer UGC videos as embedded testimonials on PDPs
  • These videos also become ad creative for Meta and YouTube pre-roll
9
No FAQ on Product Pages Medium Product Pages

The homepage has an FAQ accordion — but individual product pages have none. Common buyer questions (What room size do I need? Does it work with real balls? How accurate is the spin data? What's the setup time?) go unanswered at the exact moment someone is deciding whether to add to cart. Unanswered questions = abandoned cart.

Recommended Fix
  • Add a product-specific FAQ section to each PDP with 6–8 questions pulled from customer service data and reviews
  • Key questions to address: room requirements, ball compatibility, setup time, accuracy vs. competitors, platform/software access, what's included vs. what needs to be purchased separately
  • FAQ content also improves SEO — these are exact queries people type into Google before buying
10
Cart Page Has No Recovery Content Medium Cart

The cart page is essentially empty — just a subtotal and a checkout button. There's no reinforcement of trust signals, no reminder of the 365-day guarantee, no financing mention, no "you're one step away" urgency, and no social proof to reassure a buyer who's having second thoughts. This is the moment buyers hesitate most — and the page offers them nothing to push through.

Recommended Fix
  • Add a trust bar to the cart with: 365-Day Guarantee · Made in USA · 4.8/5.0 Rating · Free Shipping
  • Add a financing callout: "Split into 12 payments — as low as $XXX/month"
  • Show a single strong customer quote in the cart sidebar
  • If shipping is free, say it prominently — if it isn't, this is likely where sticker shock hits and people leave
11
Navigation Labels Are Confusing for New Visitors Medium Global Nav

The primary nav: Shop | Tech | Platform | RSG Club. "Tech" and "Platform" are insider language — a first-time visitor doesn't know what they'll find there. This increases cognitive load and reduces the chance someone clicks into the right funnel. Clear navigation is especially important since RSG is a relatively new brand.

Recommended Fix
  • Consider: Shop Simulators | How It Works | RSG Club | About
  • "How It Works" covers both Tech and Platform in language a prospect understands
  • Test whether adding a "Build Your Room" or "Compare Simulators" nav item increases engagement from new visitors
12
No Urgency or Scarcity Signals Medium PDPs + Cart

Nothing on the site creates a reason to act today rather than tomorrow. For a product where the consideration cycle is weeks or months, the absence of any urgency — seasonal pricing, limited availability, lead time for delivery, upcoming price changes — means buyers feel comfortable delaying indefinitely.

Recommended Fix
  • Add lead time messaging: "Orders placed before [date] ship within X business days" — real, not fake urgency
  • For seasonal peaks (Q4, spring golf season), use countdown timers tied to actual promotion windows
  • Social proof signals: "X people are viewing this right now" or "X sold this month" (Shopify apps handle this)
  • If there are genuine stock constraints on any SKU, surface them: "Only 4 left in stock"
✓
What's Already Working Strengths

Several trust elements are in place and just need to be amplified: 4.8/5.0 rating is legitimate social proof. The 365-day guarantee is industry-leading. Made in America positioning is a real differentiator. Major retail partners (Costco, Dick's, Best Buy) lend significant credibility. Military/First Responder discounts show values alignment. Shopify as the platform means all recommended fixes are either built-in or app-based — no dev sprints needed.

SEO Audit

Search Engine Optimization

Current State: Starting Fresh After a Rebrand

The rebrand from OptiShot Golf to Red Stakes Golf in late 2024, with rsgolf.com going live in March 2025, effectively reset the organic search clock. Years of domain authority, backlink equity, and brand search volume built under "OptiShot" are now pointing at a different domain. That's the bad news. The good news: rsgolf.com is ~13 months old, the site is on Shopify (technically sound), and the product category has strong organic search demand. SEO is a 12–18 month compounding investment here — but the strategy needs to be right from the start to avoid repeating the lost equity.

Brand equity risk: Anyone searching "OptiShot Golf" today — former customers, press coverage, word-of-mouth referrals — lands on a dead or competitor-controlled experience. RSG should own that traffic. A redirect strategy and content that bridges the rebrand ("formerly OptiShot Golf") will recapture some of this lost volume.

SEO Findings & Recommendations

S1
Recapture OptiShot Brand Traffic Immediately Critical SEO

"OptiShot golf" still gets 6,600+ monthly searches. These are people who've heard of the brand — warm prospects. Right now, that traffic is going to a dead domain or competitors who rank for OptiShot comparison searches. This is the single fastest SEO win available.

Recommended Fix
  • Confirm optishotgolf.com permanently redirects to rsgolf.com (301 redirects)
  • Create a dedicated page: "OptiShot Golf is now Red Stakes Golf — Here's What Changed" — this captures the search intent and tells the brand story
  • Reference "formerly OptiShot Golf" in site copy, About page, and FAQ
  • Run Google Ads on "OptiShot golf" branded terms to capture this traffic while organic authority builds
S2
Content Strategy: Cluster Model Around Buyer Journey High SEO

A 13-month-old domain needs content volume to build topical authority. The site has a blog but no evidence of a systematic cluster strategy. Without it, blog posts are orphaned and don't reinforce each other — or the product pages they should be linking to.

Recommended Fix
  • Pillar pages for core themes: "Home Golf Simulator Guide," "Golf Simulator Room Setup," "Golf Simulator Buying Guide"
  • Cluster articles targeting long-tail variants: "What size room do you need for a golf simulator?", "Golf simulator cost breakdown," "Best golf simulator for a 10x12 room," "Golf simulator vs. driving range: is it worth it?"
  • Each cluster article should link back to the relevant product page and pillar — this builds page authority and guides readers toward purchase
  • Target keyword difficulty under 40 for all new content — rsgolf.com doesn't yet have the authority to rank for KD 60+ terms
  • Publish at minimum 8–12 articles per month for the first 6 months
S3
Competitor Comparison Pages Are a Top Priority High SEO

Buyers at the bottom of the funnel compare before they buy. Searches like "Red Stakes Golf vs Full Swing," "RSG One vs SkyTrak," "OptiShot vs Trackman" have low KD and high buying intent. These pages don't currently exist — and competitors may start ranking for "Red Stakes Golf vs" queries before RSG does.

Recommended Fix
  • Build dedicated comparison pages for the top 4–5 competitors: Full Swing, SkyTrak, Trackman, Foresight Sports, Bushnell Launch Pro
  • Be honest and specific — these pages work when they acknowledge competitor strengths and clearly articulate why RSG wins on: price, Made in USA, guarantee, gamification
  • These pages also serve as paid ad landing pages for competitor keyword campaigns
S4
Product Pages Need Schema Markup & Meta Optimization Medium SEO

Product pages should have structured data (Product schema with reviews, price, availability) so Google can show rich results — star ratings, price ranges — directly in search results. This improves click-through rate significantly, especially when competing against paid ads. Shopify makes this relatively straightforward with apps or theme customization.

Recommended Fix
  • Audit current schema implementation using Google's Rich Results Test
  • Ensure Product, Review, and BreadcrumbList schema are correctly implemented on all product pages
  • Write unique, keyword-optimized meta descriptions for all major product and collection pages — Shopify defaults to product descriptions, which are rarely optimized for CTR
  • Add FAQ schema to product pages once FAQ sections are added (Issue #9) — this can generate Google SERP features
S5
Build for AI/LLM Visibility (AEO) Medium SEO

ChatGPT, Perplexity, Claude, and Google's AI Overviews are increasingly answering "what's the best home golf simulator" directly — without a click. Being cited in those answers is the new first page of Google. The content strategy above (detailed, authoritative, comparison-focused) is exactly what LLMs cite. This isn't a separate channel — it's a byproduct of great content.

Recommended Fix
  • Structure content to directly answer common questions: use H2s like "Is Red Stakes Golf worth it?" and answer them honestly and in detail
  • Add an author bio and publish dates to all blog content — LLMs weight authoritativeness signals
  • Build backlinks from golf publications, YouTube channels, and trade press — these are the sources LLMs trust most
  • Monitor AI visibility using tools like Brandwatch or manually checking ChatGPT/Perplexity for "best home golf simulator" monthly

Google Ads Strategy

A1
Dedicated Landing Pages Are Non-Negotiable Critical Google + All

Sending paid traffic to product pages that weren't built for cold ad traffic is burning budget. A product page is designed for someone already interested in that specific SKU. A paid ad landing page needs to do something different: address the objection in the ad, build trust fast, guide the visitor toward an action (purchase or lead capture), and have a clear, single CTA. Without dedicated landing pages, quality scores suffer and conversion rates stay low.

Recommended Fix
  • Build dedicated landing pages for each major campaign type: brand story/trust, specific product (RSG One, RSG Pro), competitor comparison (vs. Full Swing, vs. SkyTrak), and financing/value angle
  • Each landing page should: headline matching the ad copy, above-fold financing offer, 365-day guarantee prominent, video, 3 key social proof quotes, comparison table, single CTA
  • Remove the global navigation from landing pages — no escape routes, one decision to make
  • Use Unbounce or Replo (Shopify-native landing page builder) for faster iteration
A2
Google Campaign Structure: TOFU, Mid-Funnel & Competitor High Google Ads

Google is the main TOFU engine. High intent lives here. The campaign structure needs to reflect the buyer journey and the economics of each keyword tier — "golf simulator" is expensive but mandatory for brand visibility; "golf simulator for home" is where efficiency lives.

Campaign Structure
  • Brand campaign: "Red Stakes Golf" + "RSG One" + "rsgolf.com" — protect brand terms, lowest CPC, highest ROAS
  • OptiShot legacy campaign: "OptiShot golf," "OptiShot simulator" — recapture existing brand awareness at low cost
  • Core category: "golf simulator," "indoor golf simulator" — expensive, necessary for visibility, maximize ad extensions
  • High-efficiency MOFU: "golf simulator for home," "home golf simulator setup," "best golf simulator under $5000" — better CPL, closer to purchase
  • Competitor campaigns: "Full Swing simulator," "SkyTrak golf," "Trackman simulator" — dedicated landing pages per competitor, strong Made in USA + guarantee angle
  • Use callout extensions: "365-Day Money Back," "Made in America," "From $3,000," "Free Consultation"
  • Use sitelink extensions: Compare Simulators, See Customer Setups, Get Financing, Book a Consultation

Meta Ads Strategy

A3
Meta Is MOFU + Retargeting — Not Cold Acquisition High Meta Ads

The current pivot away from Meta cold traffic is the right call. Meta doesn't have the same purchase intent signals as Google — people aren't searching for golf simulators, they're scrolling. Cold Meta campaigns for a $3K+ product rarely work without first building desire. Meta's strength here is reaching warm audiences and staying in front of people who've already shown interest.

Meta Campaign Architecture
  • MOFU — Warm Audiences: Website visitors (30, 60, 90 day), video viewers (50%+), email list upload (496K contacts — segment by engagement), Instagram engagers
  • BOFU — Retargeting: Product page visitors, add-to-cart abandoners, checkout abandoners — these audiences need the most conversion-focused messaging
  • 4 retargeting creative angles (run simultaneously, don't pick one):
    1. Social proof: Customer testimonials, UGC room tours, "X golfers made it their best investment"
    2. Guarantee: "Try it for a full year. Return it if you don't love it." — objection killer
    3. Financing: "Own a Red Stakes Golf Simulator from $XXX/month" — removes price barrier
    4. Made in USA + brand story: "The only American-made simulator. Built in Michigan since 2005."
  • Anyone hitting product pages gets served heavy social proof — specific customer reviews, room photos, video walkthroughs
  • Lookalike audiences built from: purchasers, top 10% email engagers, 95% video viewers

LinkedIn & Bing

A4
LinkedIn: Test With Discipline, Scale Only on Data Medium LinkedIn

LinkedIn CPMs are 3–5× higher than Meta. That's the cost of precision targeting. The upside: job title targeting reaches high-income professionals, country club directors, golf course managers, and commercial real estate buyers who have the budget for a $7K–$12K setup. Kevin mentioned getting conversions here already — that's a signal worth following carefully.

LinkedIn Approach
  • Target by job title: Golf course manager/director, country club GM, commercial real estate developer, high-income titles (VP+, Partner, Owner) in relevant industries
  • Two objectives to test: Lead Gen Forms (for consultation bookings — lower friction) vs. Traffic to landing pages
  • Lead Gen Forms will outperform traffic campaigns on LinkedIn for a high-ticket product — LinkedIn users don't want to leave the platform
  • Track CPL obsessively. If CPL exceeds $200–$250 (given $3K AOV), pause and reassess before scaling
  • Creative angle: "Bring the course to your office building" — B2B/commercial angle for venue operators
  • Keep monthly LinkedIn budget capped at $2–3K until CPL data justifies increase
A5
Bing: Mirror Google, Lower Budget, Don't Ignore Low Bing Ads

Bing is the right call. The Bing audience skews older, wealthier, and Windows-based — which maps well to the RSG buyer profile. CPCs are 20–40% lower than Google for the same terms. It's not where the volume is, but it's an efficient supplemental channel.

Bing Approach
  • Import top-performing Google campaigns directly into Microsoft Ads — minimal extra work
  • Allocate 8–12% of total Google spend to Bing initially and watch performance
  • Brand + competitor campaigns are especially efficient on Bing — lower competition means lower CPCs for the same intent

Open Questions — Need Answers Before Finalizing Ad Strategy

Does RSG offer financing? (Shop Pay Installments / Affirm / Splitit) This is the single most impactful variable in the entire paid strategy. If financing exists or can be enabled, "from $X/month" becomes the headline of every ad and landing page. If not, setting it up is priority #1 before scaling spend.
What is the revenue split between eComm (direct Shopify) and lead-gen/sales-assisted? This has a drastic impact on conversion tracking, ROAS calculation, and where to focus ad budget. If most revenue closes through a sales conversation (not direct checkout), the ad goal should be driving consultation bookings — not add-to-carts.
What CRM data is available? Can we track leads from ad click through to closed deal? Without CRM visibility, any "lead gen" campaign is flying blind. We need to know which ad channels and audiences are generating actual revenue — not just leads or clicks.
Is the 365-day guarantee in all current ads, landing pages, and email flows? If not, this is a quick win across every channel. The guarantee should be in every ad headline, landing page hero, cart, and abandonment email — it's the primary objection killer for a purchase of this size.
