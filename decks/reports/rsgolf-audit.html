The Big Picture

Red Stakes Golf has rebuilt itself into a genuinely compelling brand — the only American-made golf simulator, a 365-day money-back guarantee, 4.8-star ratings, major retail and brand partnerships (Costco, Dick's, Best Buy, Anheuser-Busch), and a gamified platform that separates it from every competitor in the space. The foundation is strong. The problem is conversion.

Kevin shared the number directly on our call: roughly 300 people add to cart per period, and a fraction of a percent make it to checkout. On a $3,000 average order, closing even a portion of that gap is worth tens of thousands of dollars per month at current traffic levels — before scaling a single dollar of ad spend. Across all three channels, the theme is the same: strong brand, underdeployed trust signals, and a funnel that isn't built for a high-consideration buyer.

A 0.05% cart-to-checkout rate is not a normal Shopify outcome. Industry benchmarks put cart-to-checkout at 40–60% for healthy stores. Even underperforming stores see 15–20%. Something structural is interrupting the purchase decision — and it's costing RSG significant revenue at current traffic levels alone.

Critical Issues

1
No Financing Options on a $3,000–$12,100 Product Critical Site-Wide

No buy-now-pay-later or installment option exists anywhere on the site — not in the hero, product pages, cart, or ads. A buyer mentally blocked at the sticker price never sees a monthly payment alternative and leaves. For a product in this price range, financing messaging is table stakes — and its absence is likely the largest single contributor to cart abandonment.

2
365-Day Guarantee Is Buried — Not a Hero Element Critical Homepage + PDPs

A year-long money-back guarantee is arguably the strongest objection killer in this category — and it currently lives in a trust badge row below the fold. For a $3K–$12K purchase decision, this guarantee deserves to be a headline feature on the hero, product pages above the fold, the cart, and every retargeting touchpoint. Right now it exists but doesn't do the heavy lifting it could.

3
No Real Social Proof on Product Pages Critical Product Pages

Product pages show "Avg. Customer Rating 4.8 / 5.0" — a number, not a story. There are no individual review quotes, no real customer photos, no video testimonials. On a $3,000+ purchase, people don't trust aggregate scores — they need to see people like them who made the same decision and don't regret it. The 4.8 rating is a legitimately strong asset that's being completely underdeployed.

High Priority Issues

4
Hero CTA "Visit The Pro Shop" Doesn't Match Buyer Intent High Homepage

The homepage hero asks first-time visitors to "Visit The Pro Shop" — a passive, low-intent prompt that doesn't speak to what a cold prospect needs. Someone who landed from a Google search for "home golf simulator" wants guidance, not a browse. The CTA language and structure aren't creating the next step for the right buyer.

5
No Lead Capture for the 90% Not Ready to Buy Today High Homepage + PDPs

A $3,000–$12,000 purchase has a 30–90 day consideration cycle. The vast majority of site visitors aren't ready to buy today — they're researching. Right now there is no quiz, email capture, lead magnet, or consultation entry point to capture those people. With 496K contacts already in their database, RSG clearly understands email value — the site just isn't building the list.

6
Add to Cart Is Buried Below a Consultation Section High Product Pages

On the RSG One product page, a "Get A Free Consultation" section with a team photo and phone number appears before the Add to Cart button — meaning a buyer ready to purchase has to scroll past a sales section to take action. The consultation option is a smart tool for high-ticket buyers, but its placement is actively interrupting the purchase flow.

7
No "Help Me Choose" Experience Across a $3K–$12K Range High Collections + PDPs

RSG One vs. RSG Pro. Launch Monitor vs. Starter vs. Studio Edition. Prices ranging from $3,000 to $12,100. A first-time buyer has no guided framework for understanding which product is right for them — and confusion kills conversions. The site lists products but doesn't guide the decision.

Medium Priority Issues

8
No Video on Product Pages Medium Product Pages

Buying a $3,000+ simulator without seeing it in action is a significant leap of faith. Static images don't communicate the software experience, setup process, shot tracking, or what the room actually looks like. Video is the closest a buyer can get to an in-person demo — and it's absent on every product page.

9
No FAQ on Product Pages Medium Product Pages

The homepage has a FAQ accordion, but individual product pages have none. Unanswered questions at the point of decision — room size requirements, ball compatibility, setup time, accuracy, what's included — are a leading cause of cart abandonment on high-consideration products.

10
Cart Page Offers No Reassurance at the Moment of Hesitation Medium Cart

The cart is a bare-minimum page — subtotal, checkout button, done. No trust signals, no guarantee reminder, no financing option, no social proof. For a buyer who's second-guessing a $3,000+ purchase, this is the highest-anxiety moment in the funnel and the page gives them nothing to push through it.

11
Navigation Uses Insider Language New Visitors Don't Understand Medium Global Nav

The primary nav — Shop | Tech | Platform | RSG Club — uses terms that mean something internally but not to a first-time visitor. "Tech" and "Platform" create friction and reduce the likelihood someone clicks into the right funnel, particularly critical for a relatively new brand still building awareness.

12
No Urgency or Reason to Act Today Medium PDPs + Cart

Nothing on the site creates a reason to act now rather than later. For a product with a weeks-long consideration cycle, the absence of any urgency — delivery lead times, seasonal availability, upcoming changes — means buyers feel comfortable indefinitely delaying their decision.

✓
What's Already Working Strengths

RSG has real assets in place that just need amplification: a 4.8/5.0 customer rating, an industry-leading 365-day guarantee, a clear Made in America story, major retail validation (Costco, Dick's, Best Buy), and strong brand partnerships. The Shopify platform foundation is technically solid. The brand has everything it needs to convert at a much higher rate — the gaps are primarily about how and where these proof points are deployed in the buyer journey.

Search Engine Optimization

Starting Fresh After a Rebrand

The transition from OptiShot Golf to Red Stakes Golf — incorporated in December 2024, with rsgolf.com live in March 2025 — effectively reset the organic search clock. Years of domain authority, backlinks, and brand search volume built under "OptiShot" are now attached to a different domain. rsgolf.com is 13 months old. The product category has strong organic demand. But the strategy needs to be right from day one, or RSG risks repeating the same lost equity in three years.

Brand equity risk: "OptiShot golf" still drives thousands of monthly searches from people who've heard of the brand — warm, high-intent prospects. Right now, that traffic is landing on a dead domain or being captured by competitors who rank for OptiShot comparison queries. This is recoverable, but it needs to be addressed deliberately.

Key SEO Findings

S1
Legacy OptiShot Brand Traffic Is Being Lost Critical SEO

"OptiShot golf" continues to generate significant monthly search volume from warm, brand-aware prospects. With the rebrand complete, that traffic currently lands on a dead domain or gets captured by competitors running OptiShot comparison pages. This is the fastest, most cost-effective SEO win on the table — recapturing awareness that RSG already paid to build.

S2
No Systematic Content Strategy to Build Topical Authority High SEO

A 13-month-old domain needs content volume and structure to establish topical authority in the golf simulator space. The blog exists but there's no evidence of a pillar-and-cluster architecture that builds page authority and routes readers toward product pages. Without it, individual posts are orphaned assets that don't compound.

S3
No Competitor Comparison Pages — High-Intent BOFU Gap High SEO

Bottom-of-funnel buyers compare before they buy. Searches like "RSG One vs SkyTrak," "Red Stakes Golf vs Full Swing," and "OptiShot vs Trackman" carry the highest purchase intent in the category — and RSG currently has no content targeting any of them. Competitors could start ranking for "Red Stakes Golf vs" queries before RSG does.

S4
Product Pages Missing Structured Data for Rich Results Medium SEO

Without proper schema markup (Product, Review, FAQ), product pages can't display star ratings, pricing, and availability directly in Google search results. Rich results significantly improve click-through rates — especially important when competing against paid ads for the same search terms. Shopify makes this solvable, but it requires verification and likely some configuration work.

S5
No Strategy for AI/LLM Visibility Medium SEO

ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google's AI Overviews are increasingly answering "what's the best home golf simulator" directly, without a click. Being cited in those results is the new first page of Google — and it favors brands with authoritative, comparison-rich content and strong backlink profiles from trusted publications. RSG's rebrand means it's starting from zero in the eyes of LLMs.

Paid Ads Audit

Paid Advertising Strategy

Current State: Good ROAS, Structural Gaps

RSG is running ~$20K/month across Meta, Google, LinkedIn, and Bing with a reported 10× ROAS — strong performance at a lean spend. The challenge is that the current setup is optimized for direct response, but this product requires a full-funnel architecture. A $3,000–$12,000 purchase is not an impulse buy. The ads need to be built around earning trust, generating leads, and moving prospects through a 30–90 day consideration window — not just sending traffic to product pages and hoping for a sale.

Google Ads Strategy

A1
Dedicated Landing Pages Are Non-Negotiable Critical All Channels

Sending paid traffic to product pages that weren't designed for cold visitors is burning budget. A product page works for someone already interested in a specific SKU. A paid landing page needs to do something different — address the ad's promise, build trust quickly, handle objections, and drive a single clear action. Without dedicated pages, Quality Scores suffer and conversion rates stay artificially low regardless of how good the ads are.

A2
Google Campaign Architecture: TOFU, Efficiency, and Competitor Tiers High Google Ads

Google is the primary TOFU engine — high intent lives here. The campaign structure needs to reflect the full buyer journey and the economics of each keyword tier. "Golf simulator" is expensive but necessary for brand visibility. "Golf simulator for home" is where efficiency lives. Competitor campaigns targeting Full Swing, SkyTrak, and Trackman are a priority and require their own dedicated landing pages. Brand terms (Red Stakes Golf, RSG One) and legacy brand terms (OptiShot) should be protected in separate campaigns. Ad extensions — 365-day guarantee, Made in USA, financing, free consultation — need to be in every campaign.

Meta Ads Strategy

A3
Meta Is MOFU + Retargeting — Four Creative Angles Running Simultaneously High Meta Ads

Meta's strength isn't cold acquisition for a $3K product — it's staying in front of warm audiences while they're in the consideration window. The pivot away from cold traffic is correct. From here, Meta campaigns should run against website visitors, video viewers, email list uploads, and product page visitors — with four distinct creative angles running in parallel rather than one ad doing all the work:

  • Social proof: Real customer testimonials, UGC room tours, specific outcomes
  • Guarantee: "Try it for a full year — return it if you don't love it"
  • Financing: "Own a Red Stakes Golf Simulator from $X/month"
  • Brand story: "The only American-made simulator. Built in Michigan since 2005."

Anyone who has visited a product page should see heavy social proof ads. Lookalike audiences should be built from purchasers and top email engagers — not cold interest targeting.

LinkedIn & Bing

A4
LinkedIn: Test With Discipline, Scale Only on Data Medium LinkedIn

LinkedIn CPMs run 3–5× higher than Meta — but the targeting precision is unmatched. Job title targeting can reach high-income professionals, country club directors, golf course managers, and commercial real estate buyers who have the budget for a $7K–$12K setup. Kevin mentioned early conversions here, which is a signal worth following carefully. The right approach: lead gen forms over traffic campaigns (LinkedIn users don't want to leave the platform), a commercial angle ("Bring the course to your venue"), and a hard CPL cap before any budget increases. Start tight, prove the unit economics, then scale.

A5
Bing: Mirror Google, Capture Efficient Supplemental Volume Low Bing Ads

Bing's audience skews older, wealthier, and Windows-based — a strong fit for the RSG buyer profile. CPCs run 20–40% below Google for the same terms. Import top Google campaigns directly into Microsoft Ads, start at 8–12% of Google spend, and watch performance. Brand and competitor campaigns are especially efficient here — lower competition means lower CPCs for the same high-intent searches.

Open Questions — Needed Before Finalizing Ad Strategy

Does RSG offer financing? (Shop Pay Installments / Affirm / Splitit) The single most impactful variable in the entire paid strategy. If financing exists or can be enabled, "from $X/month" becomes the headline of every ad and landing page. If not, enabling it is priority one before scaling spend.
What is the revenue split between direct eComm and sales-assisted closing? This has a drastic impact on conversion tracking, ROAS calculation, and where to focus budget. If most revenue closes through a sales conversation rather than direct checkout, the ad objective should be consultation bookings — not add-to-carts.
What CRM data is available, and can we track from ad click to closed deal? Without CRM visibility, lead gen campaigns are flying blind. We need to connect which ad channels and audiences are generating actual revenue — not just clicks or leads.
Is the 365-day guarantee currently featured in all ads, landing pages, and email flows? If not, this is a quick win across every channel. The guarantee is the primary objection killer for a purchase of this size and should be in every headline, landing page hero, cart, and abandonment sequence.
