Before you start: mindset

The one rule that overrides everything else

On the Ayoub walkthrough, Shevy said directly: "I don't want to give them free IP... anything that resembles spec work, where they feel like they're getting something for free, and then they can take it and run with it." He asked for a version that shows the problems and withholds the fix. This deck was built that way from the start: findings and impact only, no WordPress/Divi/plugin how-to. Do not improvise implementation detail on the call, even if asked directly. That is the paid engagement.

This is your walkthrough to present the findings and earn the work to fix them. A few principles:

  • Lead the room. Do not open by asking what questions they have. Present your view first; invite questions at natural checkpoints.
  • Talk in dollars and trust, not jargon. Shevy and his team care about trade signups, sample requests, and time-to-fix, not LCP seconds. Land every number as "here is what this is costing you."
  • Position your team as the people who found this and can fix it. This was a hands-on, page-by-page review plus competitor benchmarking, not just an automated scan. Say so.
  • Show the problem and the impact. Hold the detailed how. Findings and priority, not the fix recipe. See the rule above.
  • Shevy has likely heard this exact format before (he sat through the Ayoub CRO walkthrough). He explicitly valued that a later presenter's info matched what he'd already been told: "Melissa pretty much said all the same things, so I didn't learn anything new." Consistency reads as competence to him. If you're not the person who briefed him last on Adriana Hoyos, sync messaging first. If he says "we know the format, just go," skip the pillar explainer and jump straight to findings.
  • It is fine to say "good question," or "I don't know yet, let me get back to you." On Ayoub, the single best-received moment of the whole call was Deanna admitting she didn't have a ready answer on a tracking/data-source question and committing to follow up, rather than improvising. Olga's reaction: "I think this was excellent... very comprehensive."
Pre-call checklist
  • Findings deck open and full-screen, slide 1 ready. Backup tab on the live URL.
  • This guide open on your second screen or phone.
  • Phased hours/cost ballpark ready, broken out by Phase 1 / 2 / 3, not just a single number. On Ayoub, Shevy immediately asked for the comprehensive "you guys do everything" option and wanted it split by phase so he could decide what to keep in-house and what to hand to the client's leadership for budget approval. Expect the same here.
  • Know that GA4 is not connected for this site. This audit is PageSpeed + a full hands-on browse + competitor benchmarking (Caracole, Bernhardt, Artefacto), not a traffic pull. Say this early and confidently, don't let it surface as a gap you get caught on.
  • Know your one ask: a yes to move forward, phased by effort, with Phase 1 startable almost immediately.

The 90-second story (the spine of the whole deck)

The photography and the 3D product experience are genuinely premium, but almost everything between the click and the yes is unfinished. The homepage takes 33 seconds to show its hero image on mobile. The message never states what the brand is, who it serves, or why to choose it. And the single easiest first step for a new prospect, requesting a fabric sample, is a locked login wall with zero explanation. This audit found 28 specific, ranked issues across 6 pillars, 4 of them critical. Most of Phase 1 is copy, content, and settings, not a rebuild. The biggest unlock is the trade funnel: right now it asks for credentials before it ever sells the program.

The three beats, in order:

  • 1. Where the site stands (slides 1–3): premium visuals, but the technical foundation fails before anyone gets to see them; no GA4 yet, so this audit is grounded in a hands-on review and competitor benchmarking, not traffic data.
  • 2. What is leaking and why (slides 4–15): six pillars, each with findings and what they're costing. The heart is slide 11: the trade gate.
  • 3. What to do about it (slides 16–18): every issue ranked by impact, phased execution, then how we help them fix it.

Data → action

The framing that carries the whole deck starts here: the site can't prove what it's costing you until it's measured, and it can't be measured well until GA4 is connected — but you don't need GA4 to know a 33-second hero load and a 4x-poor layout shift are losing people. Action: performance work starts regardless; GA4 connection is a parallel recommendation, not a blocker.

If asked: what do LCP and CLS mean?

LCP (Largest Contentful Paint): how long until the main thing on the page, here usually the hero photo, actually shows up. Plain version: how fast the page looks loaded. Good is under 2.5 seconds; the homepage is at 33. CLS (Cumulative Layout Shift): how much the page jumps around while it loads, text and buttons shifting as images pop in. It's a stability score, not seconds. Good is under 0.1; the contact page is at 1.001.

If asked: is this number a Google penalty or just UX?

"Both. Core Web Vitals are a confirmed Google ranking signal, so slow pages also surface lower in search, and on top of that, most visitors leave a mobile page that takes more than 3 seconds. At 33 seconds, you're not in 'ranks lower' territory, you're in 'most people never see the hero at all' territory."

5Performance Findings

On screen

5 issues, 2 critical: homepage (39/100, 33s LCP), contact page (CLS 1.001, 23.2s LCP), ~950 KiB unused JS from overlapping analytics plugins, layout shift fails on every page tested, About Us renders blank for 8+ seconds.

Say

"Mobile speed is the weak spot sitewide. The homepage takes 33 seconds to fully paint on mobile, that's the single most urgent technical fix, because it's the first thing every visitor hits. The contact page is almost as bad and adds a severe layout-shift problem on top. We also found the site simultaneously running three overlapping analytics and pixel plugins, which is dead weight on every page load. And the About Us page, arguably your strongest storytelling page, renders completely blank for over 8 seconds due to animation timing. The good news: most of this is caching, image work, and plugin cleanup, not a rebuild."

Quick cues
  • Homepage: 33s mobile LCP = most urgent fix, first thing every visitor hits
  • Contact page: CLS 1.001 and 23.2s LCP, worst of the three
  • 3 overlapping analytics/pixel plugins running at once
  • About Us: blank for 8+ seconds, your strongest story page is invisible
  • Mostly caching, images, plugin cleanup, not a rebuild
Each finding in plain English (what it is, why it matters)
  • Homepage, 33-second mobile load: the hero, the most expensive real estate on a visual luxury site, loads last. Page weighs 6.6 MB across 160 requests. Most visitors are gone before they see the brand's photography at all.
  • Contact page, slowest and jumpiest: your primary lead-capture page scores 30/100 mobile, takes 23 seconds to paint, and visibly shifts while loading, four times Google's "poor" threshold. Someone trying to reach out gets a page that mis-taps them.
  • ~950 KiB of unused JavaScript: the site runs three overlapping tracking/pixel plugins at once, duplicating payload on every page view. Dead weight that delays everything else from appearing.
  • Layout shift fails everywhere tested: homepage, products, and contact all fail Google's stability threshold on mobile. Images and lazy-loaded sections render without reserved space, so the page jumps as content arrives.
  • About Us, blank for 8+ seconds: entrance animations combined with slow media leave your strongest brand-story page looking broken on first load. We saw a white screen twice before content appeared.
Data → action

Position this as standard performance work your team scopes and handles. Do not detail the how on the call, this is Phase 2 work. The promise: get mobile speed out of the danger zone, starting with the homepage and contact page.

If asked: is the site broken?

"No, it loads, it's heavy and inefficient on mobile. Overlapping plugins and unoptimized hero images are a common pattern on WordPress sites that have grown feature by feature. Caching and image work fix most of it."
