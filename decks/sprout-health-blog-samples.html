Why Am I Gaining Weight in Perimenopause? The Hormone Shift Behind the "Menopause Middle"

You haven't changed how you eat, but the scale keeps creeping up — and it's landing around your middle. Here's the real, science-backed reason why, and what actually helps.

You haven't changed how you eat. You're still walking, still moving, still trying. And yet the scale keeps creeping up — and the weight seems to land right around your middle. If this sounds familiar, you're not imagining it, and you're not doing anything wrong.

Weight changes during perimenopause are real, they're common, and they have a biological explanation. Here's what's actually happening in your body, and what you can do about it.

Perimenopause changes your hormones — and your hormones change your weight

Perimenopause is the transition leading up to menopause. It can start in your early 40s (sometimes sooner) and last for years. During this time, your levels of estrogen don't simply drop — they swing up and down unpredictably before settling lower.

That matters for your weight, because estrogen helps decide where your body stores fat. When estrogen is higher, your body tends to store fat around the hips and thighs. As estrogen declines, fat storage shifts toward your abdomen.

Researchers have measured this shift directly. In a longitudinal study following women through the menopausal transition, visceral fat (the deeper belly fat that wraps around your organs) increased significantly as women moved through menopause, alongside a measurable drop in energy expenditure — meaning your body burns fewer calories at rest than it used to (Lovejoy et al., International Journal of Obesity, 2008).

It's not just estrogen — three things stack up at once

The "menopause middle" usually comes from several changes happening together:

  • Lower estrogen redirects fat toward your belly.
  • Muscle loss that comes naturally with age lowers your resting metabolism, so you burn fewer calories around the clock.
  • Sleep and stress changes — hot flashes, night sweats, and disrupted sleep can raise cortisol and increase cravings.

None of these are a willpower problem. They're a physiology problem. And that distinction matters, because the solutions are different.

What actually helps

The goal isn't to "fight your hormones." It's to work with your changing body. A few evidence-aligned places to start:

  • Protect your muscle. Strength training two to three times a week helps offset the muscle loss that slows your metabolism.
  • Prioritize protein and fiber. Both help you stay full and support muscle as you age.
  • Guard your sleep. Better sleep helps regulate the hunger hormones that drive late-day cravings.
  • Talk to a provider about your options. For some people, lifestyle changes are enough. For others — especially when weight affects health markers like blood pressure or blood sugar — a medical provider may discuss additional options, including GLP-1 medications.

When to consider talking to a provider

If your weight is climbing despite real effort, or it's affecting how you feel and function, that's a reasonable time to get a professional opinion. A provider can look at the full picture — your symptoms, your health history, your goals — and help you decide what's appropriate for you.

Semaglutide vs. Tirzepatide: How These Two Weight-Loss Medications Actually Compare

They're related, often confused, and not the same. Here's a clear, side-by-side look at semaglutide and tirzepatide — so you can have a more informed conversation with your provider.

If you've started researching GLP-1 medications, you've probably run into two names again and again: semaglutide and tirzepatide. They're related, they're often mentioned together, and they're easy to confuse. But they aren't the same — and the differences can matter for your results, your side effects, and your cost.

Here's a clear, side-by-side look so you can have a more informed conversation with your provider.

What they have in common

Both semaglutide and tirzepatide are once-weekly injectable medications. Both were first developed for type 2 diabetes and later studied for weight management. And both work, in part, by mimicking a gut hormone called GLP-1, which helps:

  • Slow how quickly your stomach empties, so you feel full longer
  • Reduce appetite and food "noise"
  • Support steadier blood sugar

This is why people on these medications often eat less without the constant hunger that makes dieting so hard.

The key difference: one hormone vs. two

This is the most important distinction:

  • Semaglutide targets one receptor — GLP-1.
  • Tirzepatide targets two — GLP-1 and GIP.

That second hormone (GIP) is why tirzepatide is sometimes called a "dual agonist." In head-to-head research, that dual action appears to translate into greater average weight loss.

What the research shows

In the STEP 1 trial, adults with overweight or obesity who took semaglutide alongside lifestyle changes lost an average of 14.9% of their body weight over 68 weeks, compared with 2.4% for placebo (Wilding et al., New England Journal of Medicine, 2021).

Tirzepatide was then compared directly against semaglutide in the SURMOUNT-5 trial. Over 72 weeks, participants on tirzepatide lost an average of 20.2% of their body weight, versus 13.7% on semaglutide — roughly 50 pounds vs. 33 pounds on average (SURMOUNT-5, New England Journal of Medicine, 2025).

  Semaglutide Tirzepatide
Targets GLP-1 GLP-1 + GIP
Avg. weight loss (trials) ~14.9% (STEP 1) ~20.2% (SURMOUNT-5)
Dosing Once weekly Once weekly
Most common side effects GI (nausea, etc.) GI (nausea, etc.)

In plain terms: on average, tirzepatide produced more weight loss than semaglutide in a direct comparison. That doesn't mean it's automatically the right choice for everyone — averages aren't individuals.

What about side effects?

For both medications, the most common side effects are gastrointestinal — nausea, diarrhea, constipation, and reduced appetite — and they're usually mild to moderate and tend to ease over time as your dose increases gradually. In SURMOUNT-5, gastrointestinal side effects led to people stopping treatment slightly more often on semaglutide (5.6%) than tirzepatide (2.7%) (Weill Cornell Medicine).

Your individual experience depends on your health history, so this is a conversation to have with a provider.

How to think about choosing

There's no single "best" medication — there's the one that fits you. A provider will typically weigh:

  • Your weight-loss goals and starting point
  • Your medical history and any other conditions
  • How your body tolerates side effects
  • Cost and what's sustainable for you long-term

A note on compounded versions

You may see lower-cost "compounded" semaglutide or tirzepatide advertised online. Important: compounded medications are not FDA-approved. The FDA does not review them for safety, effectiveness, or quality the way it does brand-name drugs. Compounded medications are prepared by state-licensed compounding pharmacies for specific patients when a provider determines it's appropriate. We break down what they cost in our guide to compounded semaglutide pricing.

How Much Does Compounded Semaglutide Cost? A Clear Breakdown for 2026

Compounded semaglutide is often far cheaper than brand-name GLP-1s — but price isn't the whole story. Here's a transparent breakdown, plus the 2026 FDA rules you should know.

If you've looked into GLP-1 weight-loss medications, you've probably had sticker shock. Brand-name options can run well over $1,000 a month without insurance. That's why many people start asking about compounded semaglutide — and the first question is almost always: what does it actually cost?

Here's a straightforward breakdown, plus the things that matter just as much as price.

First, the important part: what "compounded" means

Compounded semaglutide is prepared by a state-licensed compounding pharmacy rather than a brand-name manufacturer. This matters for two reasons:

  1. It's often more affordable than brand-name Wegovy® or Ozempic®.
  2. It is not FDA-approved. The FDA does not evaluate compounded medications for safety, effectiveness, or quality. They're prepared for specific patients when a licensed provider decides it's appropriate.

That second point is non-negotiable to understand before you spend a dollar. Lower cost should never mean lower diligence.

What compounded semaglutide typically costs

Pricing varies by provider, dose, and pharmacy, but here's a realistic frame:

Brand-name GLP-1 (Wegovy®, Zepbound®) $1,000–$2,000
Compounded semaglutide $150–$300

Watch for the hidden costs

The monthly headline price isn't the whole story. Before you commit, ask whether the price includes:

  • The provider consultation and ongoing medical oversight
  • Dose changes as you titrate up over time
  • Shipping to your door
  • Patient support if you have questions or side effects
  • Contracts or commitments — or whether you can stop anytime

An important 2026 update on compounding rules

The rules around compounded GLP-1s have changed recently, and it's worth knowing where things stand. During the national shortage of these medications, compounding was widely permitted. The FDA removed semaglutide from its shortage list in February 2025, which changed the legal landscape (FDA / Alston & Bird summary, 2025).

Compounding semaglutide is still legal under Section 503A — meaning patient-specific prescriptions written by a licensed provider when clinically appropriate — but under more restrictive conditions than before (FDA: Compounding and GLP-1 policies). A legitimate telehealth provider should be transparent about this.

How to evaluate an offer (a quick checklist)

Before you buy compounded semaglutide from anyone, confirm:

  • ✅ You'll have a real consultation with a state-licensed provider
  • ✅ The medication comes from a state-licensed compounding pharmacy
  • ✅ Pricing is transparent, with no surprise fees
  • ✅ There's ongoing support for dosing and side effects
  • ✅ The provider is upfront that compounded medications aren't FDA-approved
