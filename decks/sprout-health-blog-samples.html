How perimenopause changes your weight

You are still walking, still moving, still trying. And yet perimenopause weight gain shows up anyway, often right around your middle. If that sounds familiar, you are not imagining it, and you are not doing anything wrong. These changes are real, they are common, and they have a biological explanation.

Perimenopause is the transition leading up to menopause. It can start in your early 40s, sometimes sooner, and last for years. During this time your estrogen levels do not simply drop. They swing up and down unpredictably before settling lower.

That matters for your weight, because estrogen helps decide where your body stores fat. When estrogen is higher, your body tends to store fat around the hips and thighs. As estrogen declines, fat storage shifts toward your abdomen. Researchers have measured this directly: across the menopausal transition, visceral fat (the deeper belly fat that wraps around your organs) rises while the calories you burn at rest fall.

Why three things stack up at once

The so-called menopause middle is rarely about one thing. It usually comes from several changes happening together.

Estrogen redirects where fat is stored

Lower estrogen nudges fat toward your belly, which is why your body shape can change even when your weight on the scale barely moves. This redistribution is one of the most consistent findings in research on weight gain at menopause.

Muscle loss slows your metabolism

Starting in your 40s, you gradually lose muscle unless you actively work to keep it. Muscle is metabolically active tissue, so less of it means you burn fewer calories around the clock, even on a rest day.

Sleep and stress raise your cravings

Hot flashes and night sweats fragment your sleep. Poor sleep raises cortisol and throws off the hunger hormones that govern appetite, which makes late-day cravings stronger and harder to resist.

None of these are a discipline problem. They are physiology. That distinction matters, because the solutions are different from the eat-less, move-more advice that worked in your 30s.

What actually helps

The goal is not to fight your hormones. It is to work with your changing body. A few evidence-aligned places to start:

  • Protect your muscle. Strength training two to three times a week directly offsets the muscle loss that slows your metabolism.
  • Prioritize protein and fiber. Both help you feel full longer and support muscle as you age.
  • Guard your sleep. Cooling your bedroom and keeping a consistent schedule helps regulate the hunger hormones that drive evening cravings.
  • Move through the day. Short walks after meals help with blood sugar and add up more than one hard workout.

For some people, these changes are enough. For others, especially when weight starts affecting health markers like blood pressure or blood sugar, a provider may talk through additional options. Frequently asked questions

Is perimenopause weight gain inevitable?

Some shift in body composition is very common, but the amount is not fixed. Strength training, adequate protein, and good sleep meaningfully change the trajectory for most people, even though the underlying hormone changes still happen.

Why is the weight going to my belly specifically?

As estrogen declines, your body stores more fat in the abdomen rather than the hips and thighs. This is a normal hormonal redistribution, and it can happen even when your total weight changes very little.

Will hormone therapy fix the weight gain?

Hormone therapy can help with symptoms like hot flashes and may influence where fat is stored, but it is not a weight-loss treatment on its own. Whether it is right for you is a personal decision to make with a provider who knows your history.

What semaglutide and tirzepatide have in common

If you have started researching GLP-1 medications, you have probably run into two names again and again: semaglutide and tirzepatide. They are related, they are often mentioned together, and they are easy to confuse. But they are not the same, and the differences can matter for your results, your side effects, and your cost.

Both are once-weekly injectable medications. Both were first developed for type 2 diabetes and later studied for weight management. And both work, in part, by mimicking a gut hormone called GLP-1, which helps:

  • Slow how quickly your stomach empties, so you feel full longer
  • Reduce appetite and the constant background "food noise"
  • Support steadier blood sugar

This is why people on these medications often eat less without the relentless hunger that makes dieting so hard.

The key difference: one hormone vs. two

Here is the distinction that matters most. Semaglutide targets one receptor, GLP-1. Tirzepatide targets two, GLP-1 and a second hormone called GIP.

That second pathway is why tirzepatide is sometimes called a dual agonist. In head-to-head research, the dual action appears to translate into greater average weight loss, though it does not make tirzepatide the automatic right choice for everyone.

What the research shows

In the STEP 1 trial, adults with overweight or obesity who took once-weekly semaglutide alongside lifestyle changes lost an average of 14.9 percent of their body weight over 68 weeks, compared with 2.4 percent for placebo.

Tirzepatide was then compared directly against semaglutide in the SURMOUNT-5 trial. Over 72 weeks, participants on tirzepatide lost an average of 20.2 percent of their body weight, versus 13.7 percent on semaglutide, which works out to roughly 50 pounds compared with 33 pounds on average.

In plain terms, tirzepatide produced more weight loss than semaglutide in a direct comparison. Averages are not individuals, though, and plenty of people reach their goals on semaglutide.

What about side effects?

For both medications, the most common side effects are gastrointestinal: nausea, diarrhea, constipation, and reduced appetite. They are usually mild to moderate and tend to ease over time as your dose increases gradually. In the head-to-head trial, digestive side effects led to people stopping treatment slightly more often on semaglutide than tirzepatide.

Your individual experience depends on your health history, which is why this is a conversation to have with a provider rather than a decision to make from a chart.

How to think about choosing

There is no single best medication. There is the one that fits you. A provider will typically weigh:

  • Your weight-loss goals and your starting point
  • Your medical history and any other conditions
  • How your body tolerates side effects
  • Cost and what is sustainable for you long term

A note on compounded versions

You may see lower-cost compounded semaglutide or tirzepatide advertised online. It is important to understand that compounded medications are not FDA-approved. The FDA does not review them for safety, effectiveness, or quality the way it does brand-name drugs. Compounded medications are prepared by state-licensed compounding pharmacies for specific patients when a provider determines it is appropriate. Frequently asked questions

Is tirzepatide always better than semaglutide?

On average it produced more weight loss in a head-to-head trial, but better on average does not mean better for everyone. Tolerance, cost, medical history, and how your body responds all factor into the right choice for you.

Can I switch from one to the other?

Some people do switch, usually under a provider's guidance, because of side effects, results, or cost. Any change in medication or dose should be supervised by your provider.

How fast will I see results?

Both medications are started at a low dose and increased slowly over weeks to limit side effects, so meaningful changes typically build over months rather than days. The trial results reflect 68 to 72 weeks of treatment.

Are the compounded versions the same as the brand-name drugs?

They contain the same active ingredient but are not FDA-approved and are prepared by state-licensed pharmacies rather than the original manufacturer. A provider can explain the trade-offs for your situation.

The bottom line

Semaglutide and tirzepatide are close cousins with one meaningful difference, and the data gives tirzepatide an edge on average weight loss. What "compounded" actually means

If you have looked into GLP-1 weight-loss medications, you have probably had sticker shock. Brand-name options can run well over a thousand dollars a month without insurance. That is why many people start asking about compounded semaglutide, and the first question is almost always what it actually costs.

Compounded semaglutide is prepared by a state-licensed compounding pharmacy rather than a brand-name manufacturer. That matters for two reasons. First, it is often much more affordable than brand-name Wegovy or Ozempic. Second, it is not FDA-approved, which means the FDA does not evaluate it for safety, effectiveness, or quality. It is prepared for specific patients when a licensed provider decides it is appropriate. Lower cost should never mean lower diligence.

Watch for the hidden costs

The headline price is not the whole story. Before you commit, ask whether it includes:

  • The provider consultation and ongoing medical oversight
  • Dose changes as you titrate up over time
  • Shipping to your door
  • Patient support if you have questions or side effects

An important 2026 update on the rules

The rules around compounded GLP-1s have changed recently, and it is worth knowing where things stand. During the national shortage of these medications, compounding was widely permitted. Then the FDA removed semaglutide from its shortage list in early 2025, which changed the legal landscape.

Compounding semaglutide is still legal under Section 503A, meaning patient-specific prescriptions written by a licensed provider when clinically appropriate, but under more restrictive conditions than before. Legal analysts have tracked how the resolved shortage wound down large-scale compounding. A legitimate telehealth provider should be transparent about all of this.

How to evaluate an offer

Before you buy compounded semaglutide from anyone, confirm that:

  • You will have a real consultation with a state-licensed provider
  • The medication comes from a state-licensed compounding pharmacy
  • Pricing is transparent, with no surprise fees
  • There is ongoing support for dosing and side effects
  • The provider is upfront that compounded medications are not FDA-approved

Frequently asked questions

Why is compounded semaglutide so much cheaper than Wegovy?

Compounded versions are prepared by state-licensed pharmacies rather than the brand-name manufacturer, which removes much of the brand pricing. The trade-off is that compounded medications are not FDA-approved and are not evaluated by the FDA for safety, effectiveness, or quality.

Is compounded semaglutide still legal in 2026?

Yes, under Section 503A as a patient-specific prescription written by a licensed provider when clinically appropriate. The rules tightened after the FDA resolved the semaglutide shortage in 2025, so a reputable provider should be transparent about how they operate.

Does the monthly price include the doctor visit?

It depends on the provider. Some bundle the consultation, oversight, and shipping into one transparent price, while others bill them separately. Always ask what is and is not included before you commit.

Can I use insurance for compounded semaglutide?

Compounded medications are typically not covered by insurance, which is part of why the cash price is lower than brand-name drugs. A provider can walk you through your options.

The bottom line

Compounded semaglutide can make GLP-1 treatment far more affordable, but the right question is not only how cheap it is. It is whether the price is transparent, the pharmacy is licensed, and the provider is upfront that the medication is not FDA-approved.
