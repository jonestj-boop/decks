Transit vs. Sprinter: Which Van Should You Actually Build In?

Infinity Vans April 2026 ~1,250 words

Why the Van You Pick Changes Everything

You've done the research. You've watched the YouTube builds. You've got a rough floor plan sketched on a napkin. And now you're staring at two vans in a parking lot wondering: Transit or Sprinter?

This isn't a trivial question. The platform you choose shapes your build budget, your ceiling height, your parts costs, your resale value, and how many weekends you'll spend cursing at a ceiling panel that doesn't quite fit. Getting this right at the start saves you thousands of dollars and a lot of frustration.

At Infinity Vans, we build systems for both. We're not here to push you toward one over the other. We're here to help you make the right call for your actual life, not the Instagram version of it.

The Platforms: What You're Actually Choosing Between

The Ford Transit

The Transit is America's best-selling cargo van. The 148-inch extended wheelbase with a high roof is the sweet spot for conversions: serious interior volume, a flat load floor, and enough headroom that most people can stand without stooping. Parts are everywhere, mechanics know it, and the aftermarket has exploded around it. Ford's EcoBoost V6 is proven, serviceable, and has a growing community of owners who've documented every quirk online.

The Mercedes-Benz Sprinter

The Sprinter has been the prestige van of the van life world for a reason. The high-roof 144-inch wheelbase delivers around 6'4" of standing room, making it genuinely the tallest production cargo van you can buy. It's narrower than the Transit and carries less overall volume, but the ride quality is excellent and the diesel engine is built for serious mileage. For tall builders or anyone prioritizing interior livability, that headroom matters more than any other single spec.

The cargo volume gap is the biggest number that surprises people. The Transit, especially in the extended wheelbase, is a genuinely large van. The Sprinter is tall but narrower and shorter in overall volume. Neither is wrong. They're just optimized differently.

Real-World Builder Considerations

Aftermarket and Conversion Support

The Sprinter got to market first. Early van life builds standardized on its dimensions, so the community knowledge base runs deep. The Transit has caught up fast and in some categories surpassed it. Ford's market share means more suppliers are building Transit-specific products, including our own Transit kit, built because demand from Transit owners had become impossible to ignore.

Dealer Network and Running Costs

There are roughly 3,000 Ford dealers in the United States. If your Transit breaks down in rural Montana, you're almost certainly within 60 miles of someone who can fix it. Mercedes has around 350 U.S. dealers. Sprinter repair outside major metros often means longer wait times, parts orders, and higher labor rates. The Sprinter's diesel does deliver 2-3 MPG better fuel economy, which adds up on high-mileage travel. But factor in DEF fluid, pricier oil changes, and specialized service before running the math. Run the full numbers from platform to finishing touches before committing.

What About the ProMaster?

The Ram ProMaster earns an honest mention. It's front-wheel drive (useful in winter climates), cheaper new, and has a wide, flat floor. But the conversion ecosystem is thin, the aftermarket is sparse, and resale value lags both competitors. Infinity Vans focuses on Transit and Sprinter because that's where the tools, knowledge, and community actually exist. Our reliability rankings cover all three with real data if you want to go deeper.

Who Should Build in What

Specs don't tell the whole story. The right van depends on how you actually live and build. Here's how we think about it.

The Budget Builder

Build in: Ford Transit

Used Transits are more abundant and often cheaper than comparable Sprinters. The aftermarket is full of cost-effective components, repairs are cheaper, and you can put more money into the build rather than the platform. A well-built Transit conversion beats a compromised Sprinter conversion every time.

The Long-Haul Lifer

Build in: Mercedes Sprinter (with eyes open)

If you're planning months on the road covering serious miles, the Sprinter's diesel efficiency and livable headroom are real advantages. Keep an emergency repair fund and know your nearest Mercedes dealer. Do your homework and the Sprinter is an exceptional long-term platform.

The Weekend Warrior

Build in: Ford Transit

You want it ready Friday night and reliable Sunday. The Transit's medium roof parks in a normal garage, the gas engine is simple, and if something goes wrong mid-trip, options are nearby. Weekend warriors often overbuild. The fastest way to build is being ruthless about what you actually need versus what looks good in build threads.

The Fleet or Commercial Builder

Build in: Ford Transit

If you're outfitting multiple vans for a contracting business, mobile services, or rental fleet, the Transit wins on pure economics: cheaper acquisition, cheaper maintenance, broader mechanic availability, and fleet pricing from Ford that beats comparable Mercedes commercial programs. Browse the van build kits to see what's available for both platforms at fleet scale.

So, Which One?

The Transit wins on cargo volume, parts availability, purchase price, and serviceability. It's the practical choice for most builders, most of the time. The Sprinter wins on headroom, diesel efficiency, and long-term durability at very high mileage. For full-time van lifers who can handle the occasional higher repair cost, it's a legitimate choice.

Purchase price is only the beginning. The Sprinter's diesel economy can save a high-mileage driver real money at the pump over five years, but a single out-of-warranty dealer repair can cost $1,500-$3,000 for work a Transit would handle for half that at a local shop. If you want to dig into specs further, this in-depth comparison of the Sprinter, Transit, and ProMaster covers dimensions, engine options, and ownership considerations side by side.

Both platforms build beautifully when the systems are right and the work is done thoughtfully. The van is the starting point. What you put in it, and how you build, is where it either comes together or doesn't.
